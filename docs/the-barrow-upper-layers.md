# The Barrow — Relationship, Player State, and Voice

## The upper layers: how the world responds to you, who you become, and what you read.

These three layers sit at the top of the architecture, above landscape, habitation, and knowledge. They are tightly coupled — the relationship layer gates access to the knowledge layer based on the player state, and the voice layer reads all three to produce the text the player experiences. They are documented together because they function together.

---

## Part One: The Relationship Layer

The relationship layer tracks the dynamic connections between the player and the world's people. It sits above the knowledge and habitation layers and below the player state. Its primary function is gating — it determines what the player can learn, from whom, and when.

---

### 1. Trust

Trust is the primary currency of relationship and the bottleneck to deep knowledge. It is tracked per individual, not per settlement, culture, or region. Your trust with Breca is a specific value built through specific interactions. It has no automatic connection to your trust with the elder in the same settlement, though one may influence the other.

#### 1.1 How Trust Builds

**Presence over time.** The simplest and slowest mechanism. Repeated visits to someone's vicinity. Being there, consistently, without being a problem. This is the tarrying mechanic applied to people. Even without shared language, sustained non-threatening presence builds a baseline of familiarity that is the foundation for trust. This mirrors how trust works with wary animals, with children, with anyone who has reason to be cautious.

**Reciprocity.** Gifts given, help offered, work shared. Not transactions — contributions to a web of mutual obligation. Bringing herbs to the wise woman because you noticed what she needs. Helping Breca mend the wicker panel. Carrying a load for someone on the path. Each act of reciprocity deposits into the relationship. The value of the act depends on what's given relative to what's needed — a pot of rendered fat means more in a hungry winter than in an abundant autumn.

**Shared experience.** Going through something together. Sheltering from the same storm. Witnessing the same event. Being present at the same ritual. Shared experience builds trust faster than either presence or reciprocity alone because it creates a common reference point — "we were both there" — that words alone can't replicate.

**Demonstrated understanding.** Showing that you understand something the person cares about. Recognising a symbol they value. Using a word from their language correctly. Respecting a taboo without being told. Demonstrated understanding signals that you've been paying attention, that you take their world seriously, and that you can be trusted with more.

**Being vouched for.** If a person the individual trusts tells them you're trustworthy, the initial barrier is lower. This is reputation arriving ahead of you along the relationship network — a trader who tells the next settlement "this person is worth talking to" makes your first encounter there easier. Being vouched for doesn't substitute for direct trust-building, but it accelerates the early stages.

#### 1.2 How Trust Is Damaged

**Negative encounters.** Trespass — entering a space you weren't invited into. Theft — taking something without permission. Unpredictable behaviour — acting in ways the person can't interpret. Aggression — even implied aggression, even if unintentional. A single negative encounter can undo sessions of trust-building, because trust is asymmetric — easier to damage than to build.

**Identity conflict.** The player's tattoos, god-names, spoken words, or visible cultural markers may conflict with the individual's allegiances. If you carry the hill people's mark and this person fears the hill people, your presence isn't neutral — it's threatening. In severe cases, tarrying near someone who reads your identity as hostile can make things worse with each visit. They're not getting used to you. They're getting more anxious about why you won't leave.

**Breach of taboo.** Doing something the person's culture considers forbidden — eating the wrong food in their presence, speaking a god-name that shouldn't be spoken here, entering a sacred place without invitation. Taboo breaches can be devastating to trust because they signal that you either don't understand or don't respect the culture's deepest rules. Some taboo breaches are recoverable. Some are not.

**Association.** Being seen with someone the individual distrusts, or carrying an object associated with a rival or enemy group. Association damage is indirect — you haven't done anything wrong to this person, but your connections alarm them. This is the flip side of being vouched for — a negative vouching.

#### 1.3 Trust Decay

Trust doesn't reset to zero through neglect, but it cools. A relationship that isn't maintained settles to a lower resting state over time. The wise woman remembers you after a year's absence, but the warmth has faded. You're not a stranger, but you're not quite what you were. Rebuilding from a decayed state is faster than building from scratch — the foundation remains — but it requires renewed presence and renewed reciprocity.

The decay rate depends on the depth of the relationship. A shallow acquaintance decays quickly. A deep bond decays slowly. A relationship that has involved shared experience, intimacy, or ritual participation has a very slow decay rate — these bonds persist across long absences, though they still cool somewhat.

#### 1.4 Trust and Access

Trust determines what an individual is willing to share and do:

**No trust (stranger).** Basic courtesy or wariness, depending on disposition. Willingness to trade simple goods. Willingness to share regional common knowledge (directions, basic information). Nothing personal, nothing deep.

**Low trust (acquaintance).** Willingness to share cultural common knowledge. Willingness to engage in sustained conversation within shared language limits. Willingness to accept gifts and reciprocate. The beginning of a relationship.

**Moderate trust (known person).** Willingness to share specialist knowledge. Willingness to share opinions and attitudes. Willingness to talk about other people and relationships. Willingness to share word-telling fragments if they carry any. The player begins to see this person as an individual rather than a representative of their community.

**High trust (trusted friend).** Willingness to share deep knowledge — full word-tellings, symbol interpretations, sacred site significance. Willingness to vouch for the player to others. Willingness to share needs and vulnerabilities. Willingness to ask for help. The relationship is mutual — the person trusts you and you have reason to trust them.

**Deep trust (intimate bond).** Willingness to share ritual knowledge. Willingness to share knowledge of the impossible objects if they know of them. Willingness to invite the player to witness or participate in sacred practices. Physical intimacy may be possible at this level if the relationship has that character. Deep trust is rare and represents significant investment of time and presence by the player.

#### 1.5 Trust Is Not Visible

The player never sees a trust meter, a relationship bar, or a numerical value. They read trust through the voice layer's description of the person's behaviour. Body language — whether they face you, whether they turn their back, how close they stand. Conversational tone — terse and guarded, or open and warm. What they volunteer versus what must be asked for. Whether they mention personal matters. Whether they name you or refer to you generically. The voice layer translates the trust value into observable human behaviour, and the player learns to read it.

### 2. Shared Language

Shared language is tracked per individual and represents the mutual vocabulary and understanding built through time together. It is the medium through which all verbal knowledge passes — everything someone tells you is filtered through the shared language depth.

#### 2.1 How Shared Language Builds

Shared language builds through time spent communicating with a specific person. Every encounter that involves communication — even gestural — contributes. The rate of building depends on frequency and duration of contact, the complexity of the subjects discussed, and the linguistic distance between the player and the individual (see 2.3).

Early stages: gestures, pointing, essential nouns. Concrete, immediate, physical. "She offers dried fish. She points north."

Middle stages: simple sentences, local vocabulary, the ability to discuss practical matters. "She tells you the ford is dangerous after rain."

Advanced stages: complex ideas, narrative, metaphor, emotional nuance. "She tells of Ardhu, who walked north from the sea cliffs, following the ridge track for many days."

Deep stages: the ability to discuss abstract concepts, sacred knowledge, the kind of nuanced, allusive speech that word-tellings require. This level is reached only with sustained, deep contact over many visits.

#### 2.2 Partial Transferability

Shared language is partially transferable within a language family. Time spent building communication with Breca teaches you not just her specific vocabulary but something about the underlying language — its sound patterns, its grammar, its common words. This means that when you meet someone else in Breca's region who speaks a related variety, the starting point is higher. You don't begin from zero. You begin from a base of partial comprehension that reflects your accumulated exposure to the language family.

The transfer is imperfect. You understand some of what the new person says but not all. Different words for familiar things. Different pronunciation. Idioms you haven't heard. The transfer gets you to the "simple sentences" stage faster, but the deep stages still require direct investment with the specific individual.

#### 2.3 Linguistic Distance

The degree of transfer depends on linguistic distance — how closely related the two speakers' language varieties are.

**Same settlement or near neighbours.** Almost full transfer. The language is essentially the same with minor vocabulary differences. Time spent with Breca transfers almost completely to others in her settlement.

**Same region, different valley.** Substantial transfer. Recognisably the same language with different accent, different local vocabulary, some different grammar. You understand most things on first meeting.

**Same language family, different region.** Moderate transfer. Related languages that share roots and basic structure but have diverged significantly. Some words are recognisable, some are opaque. You can communicate about basic things relatively quickly but depth takes work.

**Different language family.** No meaningful transfer. The languages share no common ancestor within the timeframe that matters. Time spent learning the chalk-south language helps you not at all with the language of the men from across the water. You begin from zero — gestures, pointing, the slow construction of mutual understanding from nothing.

The game tracks the player's accumulated exposure to each language family as a background value. This value determines the starting point for shared language with any new individual from that family. It increases with every interaction within the family and never decreases (unlike trust, shared language doesn't decay — once you've learned a language pattern, you retain it).

#### 2.4 Shared Language and the Voice Layer

Shared language depth directly affects what the voice layer produces when the player interacts with a given individual.

At low depth, the voice layer describes communication in terms of gesture, expression, and simple concrete exchange. The text is sparse — short sentences, limited vocabulary, frequent indication that meaning is being negotiated rather than flowing freely.

At high depth, the voice layer produces rich, nuanced dialogue. The person speaks in their voice — their personality, their idiom, their way of expressing things. The text feels like conversation between two people who know each other well.

The transition between these states is gradual and the player feels it as the relationship deepening. They never see a "shared language level" indicator. They simply notice that this person tells them more now, in more detail, with more subtlety — and they understand it.

### 3. Reputation

Reputation is not a single value. It's a distributed, approximate, and sometimes inaccurate body of information about the player that exists in the world independently of the player's presence.

#### 3.1 What Reputation Contains

Reputation is a collection of stories about the player, reduced to simple impressions as they propagate:

What the player is known for — their craft, their trade goods, their helpfulness or unreliability.
Where the player has been — which settlements, which regions, which sacred sites.
What the player carries — identity markers, unusual objects, notable trade goods.
What the player has done — notable actions, witnessed events, breaches of taboo.
The player's lack of lineage — always present as part of reputation until or unless it's resolved.

These elements are not transmitted as a complete profile. Each person who carries reputation knowledge holds a simplified, partial, possibly distorted version. The elder in the next settlement heard from a trader that a stranger came through Breca's settlement who was good with flint. That's all they know. The stranger's name, their tattoos, their word-telling knowledge — none of that transmitted. What transmitted was one trait, filtered through one trader's impression.

#### 3.2 How Reputation Propagates

Reputation travels along the pathways of human contact:

**Trade routes.** The primary vector. Traders carry news between settlements on their regular circuits. A trader who visited Breca's settlement carries their impression of the player to the next settlement on their route, and the next, and the next. Propagation speed is limited by the trader's walking pace and the frequency of their circuit — a route that takes a month to complete means reputation arrives roughly a month after the event.

**Inter-settlement visits.** People visit relatives, attend gatherings, make pilgrimages. Each visit is an opportunity for reputation to propagate, but these are less regular than trade routes and less predictable.

**Calendar gatherings.** When multiple settlements gather at a sacred site for a calendar event, reputation propagates rapidly among those present. A midsummer gathering at a stone circle is a reputation exchange — everyone who attended carries news of everyone else back to their home settlements.

**The arriving people's network.** The men from across the water have their own communication channels. What they learn about the player propagates through their group and potentially back across the water, though this is largely invisible to the player.

#### 3.3 Reputation Geography

Reputation does not spread in a uniform radius. It follows the paths of human contact, which means it has a specific geography.

The player might be well-known along the ridgeway they've travelled — every settlement on that route has heard of them through the traders who walk it. But the settlement in the valley just over the ridge, which trades on a different route, has never heard of the player. The player's reputation has a *shape* determined by trade routes and social connections, not by distance.

This creates interesting situations. A player might walk into a settlement expecting to be recognised and finding they're unknown, because they've crossed from one trade network into another. Or they might arrive somewhere they've never been and find their reputation preceded them along a route they didn't walk — because a trader carried it.

#### 3.4 Reputation Distortion

Reputation is imperfect by nature. Each transmission simplifies, exaggerates, omits, or misinterprets. A helpful act might be reported as weakness. A firm refusal might be reported as hostility. A gift of unusual generosity might be reported as suspicious wealth. The player has no control over how their reputation is transmitted and no way to correct it before encountering its effects.

This means the player sometimes arrives at a settlement and encounters attitudes they didn't earn — positive or negative. A settlement might be unexpectedly warm because the trader described the player favourably. Another might be unexpectedly cold because someone's impression was shaped by the player's tattoos rather than their actions. Reputation distortion is a realistic consequence of living in a world where information travels by mouth and is never perfectly accurate.

### 4. Encounter History

The relationship layer maintains a record of every significant encounter between the player and each known individual. Not a complete transcript — a compressed history of key events.

**First meeting.** When, where, under what circumstances. First impressions carry weight.

**Gifts and reciprocity.** What was given and received. What help was offered. What work was shared.

**Knowledge shared.** Which word-tellings were told, which fragments of knowledge were exchanged, what was asked and what was answered.

**Negative events.** Any breach of trust, any conflict, any misunderstanding.

**Shared experiences.** Events both the player and the individual were present for — storms, rituals, encounters with others, significant discoveries.

**Intimacy.** Whether the relationship has included physical intimacy, and the circumstances.

The encounter history informs the voice layer's portrayal of each interaction. A person the player has known for a long time and visited many times is described differently from someone met once. The history creates continuity — the voice layer can reference shared experiences, past conversations, old promises, previous gifts. This is how relationships feel real rather than stateless.

### 5. Identity Reading

Every NPC reads the player's visible identity markers and responds. Identity reading is the first thing that happens in an encounter — before trust builds, before shared language develops, the person *sees* you and forms an impression based on what's visible.

#### 5.1 What Gets Read

**Tattoos and body markings.** Which people you've lived among, which groups have marked you. Readable to anyone from the same cultural context; potentially readable, differently, by people from other contexts.

**Clothing and materials.** What your clothes are made from and how they're made tells people about your geographic origin and social status. Hide clothing is different from woven cloth. Coastal materials are different from inland materials.

**Objects carried visibly.** A fine flint blade says you're from the chalk or connected to it. A copper ornament says you have access to rare trade goods. A spiral-marked stone says you carry a symbol that some people revere and others fear. Movable symbolic objects are read immediately and seriously.

**Physical condition.** Whether you look well-fed or hungry, rested or exhausted, healthy or injured. Physical condition signals how far you've come and how hard the journey was.

**Speech patterns.** Once communication begins, your accent and vocabulary mark you. If you've spent a long time among the water-people, their cadences are in your mouth, and someone from the mountains will hear that immediately.

**God-names.** If you speak a god's name — at a shrine, in conversation, in exclamation — you've declared an allegiance. Some people will welcome it. Others will go quiet.

**The absence of lineage.** You have no known people. In a world where genealogy is credentials, this is conspicuous. Everyone reads it. Most are wary. Some are curious. A few — the outcasts, the hermits, the wise woman — might find it interesting rather than alarming, because they too stand outside the normal social structure.

#### 5.2 How Identity Reading Affects Encounters

Identity reading modifies the initial encounter state — the starting point before trust has had a chance to build. A positive reading (your markers align with things the person values) gives a warmer start. A negative reading (your markers alarm or offend) gives a colder start. A neutral reading (your markers are unknown or irrelevant) gives the default start defined by the person's disposition.

The effects can be strong enough to override disposition. A person who is normally welcoming might be hostile if your tattoos mark you as belonging to a group they fear. A person who is normally wary might be immediately open if you carry an object they recognise as sacred.

Identity reading is not a one-time event. It's ongoing. Each new marker the player acquires — a new tattoo, a new god-name spoken, a new object carried — shifts how they're read by everyone who can see it. The player's identity accumulates visually over the course of the game, and that accumulation has consequences that are distributed, unpredictable, and partially invisible. Taking the hill people's mark opens doors with the hill people and potentially closes them with everyone who mistrusts the hill people, and the player may not discover which doors have closed until they're standing in front of them.

## Part Two: The Player State Layer

The player state is the accumulation of everything the player has done, learned, seen, and become. It is the unique fingerprint of this particular life in the landscape.

---

### 6. Physical State

**Location.** Current district, current site or path, position within a cave system if underground.

**Condition.** A set of physical parameters that affect what the player can do and how the voice layer describes their experience:

Hunger — how recently and how well the player has eaten. Affects energy, travel speed, decision quality, and vulnerability to cold. Hunger is the most persistent physical pressure in the game — it recurs daily and seasonally.

Cold — how exposed the player is. Affected by weather, altitude, shelter, clothing, fire, season. Cold affects travel endurance, sleep quality, and health. Prolonged cold is dangerous.

Fatigue — how rested the player is. Affected by travel, sleep, exertion, time of day. Fatigue affects perception (the voice layer's descriptions become less detailed as the player tires), decision quality, and physical capability. Extreme fatigue feeds into convergence density.

Injury — specific damage from falls, exposure, encounters, environmental hazards. Injuries heal over time but limit what the player can do while they persist. A twisted ankle slows travel. A cut hand makes craft work difficult. Injuries are specific and described concretely by the voice layer.

Illness — sickness from bad water, exposure, contaminated food, seasonal diseases. Illness is a serious condition that requires rest, warmth, and potentially healing knowledge. A sick player in a remote area without help is in real danger.

**Altered states.** Physical and perceptual states that depart from the normal:

Exhaustion — beyond normal fatigue. The player has pushed past sensible limits. Perception shifts. The voice layer's descriptions become unreliable in specific ways — was that a figure on the ridge or a standing stone? Feeds into convergence density.

Fear — triggered by specific circumstances (darkness underground, dangerous terrain, hostile encounter, wight presence). Fear affects decision-making and feeds into convergence density.

The wise woman's preparations — ingested substances that alter perception. Duration varies. Effects vary — heightened sensory awareness, visual disturbance, time distortion, emotional intensity. Strongly feeds into convergence density. The player is vulnerable while under the influence.

Sensory deprivation — prolonged darkness, prolonged silence, prolonged isolation. Shifts perception in ways that feed into convergence density. The ritual darkness passage is the most extreme form.

**What the player carries.** Tools, food, materials, trade goods, movable symbolic objects, any impossible object entrusted to them. Carrying capacity is limited by physicality — you can carry what a person can carry on foot. Heavy loads slow travel. Fragile items can break. The game doesn't need a detailed inventory system — it needs the player to be aware of what they have, what they might need, and what they're willing to leave behind.

---

### 7. Knowledge State

Everything the player has learned, organised by the knowledge layer's categories.

**Common knowledge tags.** Universal, regional, cultural, and specialist knowledge acquired through presence and time. These are simple flags — the player knows or doesn't know each piece.

**Deep knowledge fragments.** Every word-telling heard (which version, from which teller, at which shared language depth). Every symbol instance seen (where, when, in what context). Every sacred site visited (at what time, under what conditions, what was observed). Every ritual witnessed or participated in. Every Old Tongue name heard and any resonances noted between names.

**Pattern connections.** Which deep knowledge fragments the player has connected — explicitly or implicitly. When the voice layer notes "this mark reminds you of..." and the player pursues it, that's a pattern connection forming. The system tracks the density of connections as a measure of how close the player is to Pattern.

**Cave knowledge.** The player's mental map of any cave system visited with light. This is stored as a set of known features — turnings, narrowings, chambers, distinctive wall textures — that determine whether the player can navigate the cave in darkness. The level of detail depends on how many lit visits the player has made and how attentive they were (which is reflected in how long they spent in each section of the cave).

---

### 8. Skill State

Practice levels for embodied craft activities. Not displayed numerically — expressed through what the player can make and how the voice layer describes their attempts.

**Flint-knapping.** From crude scrapers to fine leaf-shaped blades. Quality affects trade value, utility, and reputation.

**Woodworking.** From rough shelters to competent joinery. Determines what the player can build.

**Pottery.** From basic pinch pots to well-formed vessels with consistent firing. Quality affects trade value.

**Fire-making.** From laborious spark-and-tinder to confident, quick fire in most conditions. Affects survival capability.

**Herb identification.** From recognising a few common plants to understanding seasonal, regional, and medicinal properties. Connects to the knowledge layer and to trade.

**Carving.** From crude marks to controlled, deliberate symbol-making. Determines whether the player can make movable symbolic objects of quality.

**Boat-handling.** From cautious paddling to confident navigation. Determines what waterways the player can traverse and in what conditions.

**Tracking and reading.** From missing obvious signs to reading subtle animal behaviour, weather patterns, and landscape features. This is the practical expression of the Land tier in the system graph.

Each skill improves through practice — doing the activity, repeatedly, over time. Improvement is gradual, never sudden, and the voice layer describes the experience of getting better: "The edge is cleaner than your last attempt. Still rough, but the flake came away where you intended." The player feels their own improvement without ever seeing a number change.

---

### 9. Identity State

The accumulated identity markers that the world reads.

**Tattoos and markings received.** Which groups have marked the player, and what the marks signify. Irreversible.

**God-names spoken.** Which gods the player has named at shrines or in conversation. Not irreversible in principle — you can adopt new gods — but past invocations are remembered by anyone who witnessed them.

**Cultural affiliations.** Which communities the player has spent the most time with. Expressed through speech patterns, vocabulary, food preferences, material culture. These shift gradually as the player moves between communities but never fully reset — traces of earlier affiliations persist.

**Craft identity.** What the player is known for making. A player who produces fine arrowheads is identified as a flint-worker. A player who carves symbols is identified as something more unusual.

**Lineage.** Begins as "none" — the player emerged from a barrow with no people. This is a conspicuous absence that colours every encounter. Whether lineage can be acquired — through adoption, through partnership, through a word-telling that reveals the player's origin — is a question for future development, but the absence is an active element of identity throughout the game.

**Associations.** Who the player is known to spend time with. A player who is seen regularly with the wise woman is associated with her — for good or ill. A player who has been seen with the men from across the water carries that association.

---

### 10. Temporal State

**Calendar position.** Current season, current lunar phase, current time of day, current year of play. Determines what's accessible, what's growing, what the weather parameters are, and what calendar events are approaching.

**Seasons lived through.** How many complete seasonal cycles the player has experienced. Determines passive Seasons knowledge — a player who hasn't lived through a winter literally doesn't know what winter does to this world.

**Duration in world.** Total time elapsed since emergence from the barrow. This is the game's master clock. It drives aging of NPCs, population change, progressive landscape change (water-lands sinking, ice retreating), and the slow cultural change produced by the arriving people's influence.

---

### 11. Relationship State

Trust levels, shared language depths, encounter histories, and reputation state for all known individuals. Cross-references to Part One of this document.

---

### 12. Quest State

Not a quest log. Not a list of objectives. A record of which deep knowledge threads the player has engaged with — which fragments they've encountered, which connections they've made, which threads they're actively following.

The quest state has no prescriptive function. It doesn't tell the player what to do. It tells the generation system what the player is interested in, which allows the subtle knowledge-responsive bias when generating new districts. And it tells the voice layer what resonances to note — "this mark reminds you of..." only happens if the player has previously seen the mark it reminds them of.

The quest state also tracks the convergence conditions for Passage — how many pattern connections have been made, how much ritual knowledge has been assembled, whether a threshold barrow has been identified. This tracking is invisible to the player. It exists so that the game knows when Passage becomes possible, not so the player knows how close they are.

## Part Three: The Voice Layer

The voice layer is the top of the stack. It reads everything below and produces the text the player reads and the choices the player is offered. It is the game's only interface. Everything the player experiences comes through the voice layer.

---

### 13. The Fragment Library

The fragment library is a structured database of hand-authored prose, written in the game's specific literary voice. The voice is defined by:

Short sentences. Concrete nouns. Specific sensory detail. No purple prose. No exposition. No explanation of what the player should feel. Trust the reader.

"Pig tracks in the mud. The smell of smoke is stronger."
"Rain on the wind from the west, coming in sheets."
"She looks tired. She doesn't stop working."
"The stone is taller than you expected."

Fragments are organised by context and combine multiplicatively.

#### 13.1 Fragment Categories

**Terrain.** Descriptions of landscape types. Multiple variants per type. "Chalk downland, open, the grass cropped short." "The ridge runs east, white where the turf has slipped." "Limestone pavement, grey and fissured, with stunted hawthorn in the gaps."

**Weather.** Descriptions of conditions. "Rain, steady, from the southwest." "Fog in the valley — the hilltops are clear but below is white." "Cold. The kind of cold that settles in the bones."

**Season.** Descriptions of seasonal state. "New green on the willows by the water." "The bracken is brown and beaten down." "Frost on everything — the grass, the thatch, the still water."

**Time of day.** Descriptions of light and time. "Dawn. Low light on the ridge, long shadows." "Midday. The light is flat, the colours washed." "Dusk. The valley filling with shadow."

**Sensory detail.** Specific observations. Sounds — birdsong, water, wind, animal noise, human activity, silence. Smells — smoke, earth, rain, vegetation, animal, cooking. Touch — cold, warmth, wind, wet, the texture of rock or bark or earth. These are the finest-grain fragments and the ones that give the voice its distinctive concreteness.

**Site type.** Descriptions of place categories. Settlement (roundhouses, smoke, activity, animals, people). Standing stone (scale, position, marks, shadow). Cave entrance (dark opening, air quality, sound). Ford (water level, current, crossing quality). Hilltop (view, wind, exposure). Spring (water quality, vegetation, sacred associations).

**Underground.** A separate set for cave descriptions. These fragments shift the voice register — shorter, more compressed, focused on sound and touch rather than sight. "The wall is wet under your hand." "The passage narrows. You can feel the ceiling lowering." "Dripping, somewhere ahead. The echo is different here — the space is larger."

**Relational.** Descriptions of people and encounters. These vary with relationship state. At low trust: "She watches you from the doorway. Her expression gives nothing away." At high trust: "She sees you coming up the path and raises a hand. There's food by the fire."

**Observation.** Things the player notices, filtered by their knowledge state. "Off to the left, a break in the trees where the ground rises to an outcrop." "The herb the wise woman described — you recognise the leaf shape." "A mark on the stone. You've seen this mark before."

**Travel.** Fragments describing the experience of covering ground — forward momentum, distance passing, the landscape changing gradually. These are distinct from terrain fragments (which describe a place) and observation fragments (which note a specific detail). Travel fragments describe traversal.
They are tagged by geology and sometimes by terrain type (ridge, valley, forest, open ground, river-following, coast-following). They don't need weather or time tags — the assembler combines a travel fragment with separate weather and time observations.
"The ridge runs on. Chalk underfoot, wind on your left, the valley dropping away to the east."
"Slow going through the trees. The oaks press close. Every few steps, a branch to push aside."
"You follow the river bank. The water is brown and steady. Alders lean overhead."
"Open ground, easy walking. The moor stretches out in every direction, brown and green and brown."
"The path climbs. Your breath comes harder. The trees thin as the ground rises."
Travel fragments have forward momentum in their grammar — present tense verbs of motion, the landscape as something you're moving through rather than standing in. "The ridge runs on" rather than "the ridge stretches ahead." "Slow going through the trees" rather than "the trees are dense here."

#### 13.2 Combinatorial Assembly

Fragments combine to produce descriptions. A single moment of gameplay might draw from terrain + weather + season + time of day + sensory detail + site type + observation, assembled into flowing prose. Ten terrain fragments for chalk downland, times ten weather fragments for autumn rain, times ten sensory details, produces a thousand combinations before the LLM adds variation.

The voice layer selects fragments based on current state — where the player is, what the conditions are, what the player knows — and combines them. The LLM then smooths the joins, adds variation to prevent mechanical repetition, and incorporates relational content that can't be pre-authored because it depends on too many state variables.

#### 13.3 Fragment Library Scale

The library needs to be large enough that the player rarely encounters the same description twice for the same type of situation, across hundreds of sessions.

A rough estimate: eight to ten terrain types, with fifteen to twenty variants each. Ten to twelve weather conditions, with ten to fifteen variants each. Four seasons, with ten to fifteen variants each. Four or five times of day, with ten variants each. Fifty to a hundred sensory detail fragments. Eight to ten site types, with fifteen to twenty variants each. Twenty to thirty underground fragments. Thirty to fifty relational fragments across trust levels. Thirty to fifty observation fragments across knowledge levels.

Total: roughly five hundred to eight hundred hand-authored fragments, each one to three sentences long. This is a significant authoring effort but a bounded one — it's the game's literary voice, committed to text, and it's written once.

### 14. The LLM's Role

The LLM is a limited and controlled contributor. It serves the voice, not the other way around.

#### 14.1 What the LLM Does

**Smooths fragment assembly.** Joins selected fragments into flowing prose, adjusting transitions, varying word order, preventing mechanical-sounding assembly.

**Generates relational content.** What people say, how encounters unfold. This is the content most dependent on state — who's speaking, their trust level, their shared language depth, what they know, what they're willing to share, the player's identity, the encounter history. The LLM is given the current state, the fragment library's voice guidelines, and explicit constraints on what must and must not be said, and it produces the encounter text.

**Produces word-telling text.** Each word-telling delivered to the player is generated by the LLM from the core narrative structure, the variant parameters, the teller's trust level and shared language depth, and the voice guidelines. This is one of the LLM's most important functions — producing the oral-tradition voice that makes word-tellings feel like real tellings rather than data dumps.

**Varies descriptions.** Ensures that even with the same fragment inputs, the output text varies enough to feel fresh across repeated visits to similar situations.

**Generates choices.** Produces the options offered to the player at each decision point, phrased as natural impulses emerging from the situation. The choices are constrained by the current state (what's physically possible, what the player knows, what relationships allow) but the phrasing is the LLM's work.

**Generates travel narratives.** When the player covers multiple cells in a directional travel sequence, the LLM receives: the travel fragment, one or two sensory observations from the traversed cells, any notable features passed, and the terrain context. It assembles a 3-4 sentence compressed journey narrative. The travel narrative should have forward momentum — the sense of ground passing — and should incorporate the sensory observations naturally rather than listing them. The LLM must not over-narrate the journey — no dramatic incidents, no invented encounters, no emotional arc. Just the experience of walking through the landscape, compressed into a paragraph.

#### 14.2 What the LLM Does Not Do

**Invent plot.** The state and knowledge layers determine what's true. The LLM describes it.

**Decide what people know.** The knowledge layer determines knowledge. The LLM expresses it within the relationship layer's constraints.

**Generate world content.** No new sites, symbols, word-tellings, or connections. The LLM works with what exists.

**Break voice.** The LLM is constrained by explicit voice guidelines in every prompt. Short sentences. Concrete detail. No purple prose. No emotional instruction. Trust the reader. The LLM's tendency toward verbosity, abstraction, and emotional hand-holding must be actively and continuously counteracted.

#### 14.3 Voice Guidelines for the LLM

The voice guidelines are included in every LLM prompt. They define:

**Sentence length.** Short by default. Variation is permitted but the average should be under fifteen words. Longer sentences are reserved for word-tellings and for moments of high convergence where the prose intentionally shifts register.

**Diction.** Concrete nouns. Specific verbs. No abstractions where a concrete image will serve. Not "the landscape was beautiful" but "the valley was green below, the ridge white above, and a river between."

**Sensory priority.** Lead with what the player can sense — see, hear, smell, touch. Feelings and interpretations follow from sensory experience, not the other way around.

**Trust the reader.** Do not explain. Do not interpret. Do not tell the player what to feel. Describe what is. Let the player respond.

**Restraint.** Less is more. A three-sentence description that is specific and concrete is better than a paragraph that is atmospheric but vague. The game's power comes from precision, not volume.

**The convergence spectrum.** At low convergence: plain, material, specific. At moderate convergence: richer sensory detail, more atmospheric, the beginnings of ambiguity. At high convergence: the edges of material description, the liminal, the moment where perception becomes uncertain. The voice layer's register shifts with convergence density, but it never crosses into explicit supernatural statement. It always describes experience, not explanation.

### 15. Illustration Selection

The voice layer determines when an illustration appears. Illustrations are rare — most screens are text only. Their rarity makes them powerful. When an image appears, the game is saying *look at this*.

#### 15.1 When Illustrations Appear

An illustration appears when the player encounters something visually significant for the first time, or when a previously seen thing is revealed in a new way:

A standing stone, seen up close for the first time.
A symbol, carved or scratched, at a significant site.
A cave painting, in the moment when the torch reveals it.
An impossible object, in the moment of first encounter.
A landscape viewed from a summit, when the shape of the world is revealed.
A specific artefact — a bronze amulet, a carved figure, a jet bead.
A barrow entrance, at the moment of emergence or return.

Illustrations do not appear for: generic travel scenes, weather, settlement views at a distance, people (people are described in text, not depicted). The illustrations show things, not people and not events. They show the evidence — the objects and marks and landscapes that the player discovers.

#### 15.2 Selection From the Library

The voice layer selects illustrations from the curated library based on what's present at the site and what the player is encountering. The library contains several hundred drawings, tagged by subject type, geological context, and symbol content. A standing stone illustration tagged "limestone, spiral mark" is selected when the player encounters a limestone standing stone bearing a spiral.

The same illustration may appear in different contexts — the same standing stone drawing might appear at two different sites if both are limestone with spiral marks. This is intentional. The illustrations are not site-specific — they're type-specific, like book illustrations that accompany recurring themes rather than unique scenes.

#### 15.3 The Illustration Style

Spare line art in a consistent hand. Reminiscent of early twentieth-century book illustration — Arthur Rackham's ink work, Eric Ravilious's engravings, the wood-cut tradition of the private press movement. More suggested than rendered. Atmospheric rather than detailed. The illustrations should look like they were drawn by one person, with a recognisable style, creating the sense that the game is a book illustrated by someone who has been to these places and drawn what they saw.

### 16. Choice Generation

The voice layer generates the choices offered to the player. Choices are the player's only interface with the game — they are how the player acts in the world.

#### 16.1 Choice Principles

**Emerge from the situation.** Choices reflect what's physically possible, what the player knows, and what the relationship context allows. They are not a fixed menu. They change with every situation.

**Phrased as impulses.** Not game commands. Not "examine stone" or "talk to NPC." Instead: "Approach the stone." "Go to Breca." "Wait and watch." The phrasing should read as a natural thought — what the player might think to do if they were actually there.

**No highlighted option.** The game never tells the player which choice matters most. There is no hint system, no glow, no subtle emphasis on the important option. The player chooses based on what they care about.

**Variable number.** Two to five choices per decision point, depending on situation complexity. A simple travel moment offers two or three. A socially complex settlement visit might offer four or five. Never more than five — constraint creates clarity.

**Knowledge-gated choices.** Some choices only appear because of what the player knows. "Show your hands, palms open" appears because the player learned this gesture. "Trace the spiral in the dirt" appears because the player recognises the symbol. The appearance of a new choice that wasn't available before is one of the game's subtle rewards for accumulated knowledge — the world offers more to a player who knows more.

#### 16.2 Choice Types

**Movement choices.** Where to put your body. "Continue along the ridge." "Descend into the valley." "Enter the cave." "Follow the river."

**Attention choices.** What to focus on. "Examine the mark on the stone." "Watch the birds." "Listen." "Wait and see what happens at dusk."

**Social choices.** How to engage with people. "Go to Breca." "Approach the elder." "Help with the work." "Say nothing and sit with her." "Show the marked stone."

**Giving choices.** What to offer. "Give the herbs." "Leave the carved figure at the base of the stone." "Share the food."

**Risk choices.** Whether to accept vulnerability. "Cross the ford." "Enter the cave without light." "Drink what she offers." "Stay out in the storm."

**Silence choices.** Always present. "Wait." "Say nothing." "Stay here." These are never wasted options — the tarrying mechanic means that choosing to stay and be present is often the most productive thing the player can do, even though it looks like doing nothing.

**Travel choices.** Where to go and how. "Continue north along the ridge." "Follow the river downstream." "Push deeper into the trees." These are not single-step movements — they initiate multi-cell travel sequences (documented in the interface specification, section 3.4). The game moves the player along the chosen bearing, narrating the journey, until an interrupt condition (terrain change, feature sighting, hazard) stops the travel and returns control to the player. Travel choices include the mode of travel in their phrasing — "follow the river" is different from "head east" even if both go east, because the river-following choice stays on the bank while the heading-east choice crosses open ground. The phrasing sets expectations about speed, difficulty, and what the player might encounter.

### 17. Summary

The three upper layers work together to produce the player's experience.

The **relationship layer** gates access to the world's knowledge through trust, shared language, reputation, and identity reading. It tracks the dynamic connections between the player and the world's people, and it makes those connections feel real through the voice layer's portrayal of human behaviour.

The **player state** layer accumulates everything the player has done and become — physical condition, knowledge, skills, identity, relationships, temporal position, and the threads they're following. It is the unique fingerprint of this life in the landscape, and it makes the experience personal. Two players at the same site at the same time see different things, because their states differ.

The **voice layer** reads everything and produces text. It draws on the fragment library for the game's distinctive literary voice — short, concrete, spare, trusting the reader. It uses constrained LLM generation for relational content, word-tellings, and variation. It controls when illustrations appear. It generates the choices the player makes. It manages the convergence spectrum — shifting register from plain material description toward the liminal as convergence conditions accumulate.

Together, these three layers ensure that The Barrow's world is not experienced abstractly or generically. It is experienced specifically — through the particular prose that describes this hill at this time of day in this weather as perceived by this player with this knowledge and this history. The specificity is the game.
