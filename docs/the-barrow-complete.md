# The Barrow

## A game about walking, watching, waiting, and wondering what's over the next hill.

---

You emerge into a landscape. You don't know where you are. You don't know why you're here.

You're standing on a hillside in ancient Britain — a Britain that never quite existed, generated fresh each time, but built from the grammar of real landscape. Barrows on ridgelines. Settlements near fords. Holy wells where springs break through at geological boundaries. Trackways following the high ground above the dark, forested valleys. The land has a logic, and learning to read that logic is the first thing the game teaches you, though it never says so.

---

## The Fixed Frame

The world of The Barrow is roughly the scale of the British Isles — six hundred miles from the south coast to the ice front, three hundred miles east to west at the widest. The world is big enough that no player should ever see all of it. Committing to a direction is a real decision, because going north means not going west, and you can't do both in one season.

Four edges define the world, and each feels completely different.

**The south coast** is the most definite edge. Sea. You can stand on the chalk cliffs and see nothing but water. But it's not a wall — people come from across the southern sea. Men with bronze at their throats. Trade goods. Stories from places you'll never visit. The south coast is where the world arrives, and it's the most densely settled and culturally complex part of the landscape, because it's been connected to the continent longest. New people are landing here with bronze and new ideas and new gods. The tension between the existing world and the arriving world is one of the deep currents in the game.

**The west coast** is wilder. Longer. Deeply indented — estuaries, peninsulas, islands visible offshore. The Atlantic. People here are oriented toward the sea as a presence, a power, a source of weather and fish and danger. The west is where the oldest traditions persist, because it's furthest from continental influence. A major estuary or channel cuts deep inland, separating a far western peninsula — culturally distinct, hard to reach, where the oldest knowledge may survive. The rock here is old — granite, slate — and the sacred places are ancient.

**The north is ice.** Not a clean line but a ragged, shifting zone. Moraines, meltwater rivers cutting through scoured valleys, newly exposed rock with no soil yet. The edge is moving south to north — slowly enough that a player wouldn't notice in a season, but perceptibly over years. Land that was glacial rubble when you first played is starting to grow moss when you return. No human meaning here, or almost none. Perhaps the very bravest or most desperate people are pushing into the margins, following the retreating ice, finding things the ice is uncovering. The ice front is the game's edge of the map, but it's a living edge that moves.

**The east is the water-lands.** This is the most distinctive boundary, because it's not a boundary at all. It's a zone. A vast, low-lying patchwork of shallow lakes, reed beds, tidal flats, and temporary islands — the game's equivalent of Doggerland in its final, drowning stage. Channels shift with the seasons. Paths that exist in summer vanish in winter when the water rises. A great sandbank — a large, low island far out in the water-lands — persists, reachable by boat, possibly the site of something significant.

The people of the water-lands are culturally distinct from the mainland. They build on stilts, on crannogs, on raised mounds. They navigate by boat. Their knowledge is tidal and seasonal in a way that's different from the inland people — they know the channels, the safe crossings, where the water rises first. And they carry memory of land that is now under water. Their word-tellings include stories of places the player can never visit, because they're drowned. The water-lands are also sinking. Not fast enough to see day by day, but fast enough that the oldest people remember land that is now water. Fish swim through what were once doorways.

The moon governs access to the water-lands. A path that exists at low tide vanishes at high tide. A player who learns to navigate them — by building relationships with the water-people, by learning the tidal rhythms, by understanding the moon's effect on water levels — can move east far beyond the mainland coast. And what's out there? Other islands? A connection to the continent that's still just barely traversable at the right tide in the right season? That's a discovery for the player to make.

**The interior.** Between these four edges, the mainland itself. A mountainous spine running roughly north-south through the western half — old, worn, rounded, boggy on top. Not dramatic peaks, but high enough to create a meaningful divide between the western coastal cultures and the eastern lowlands. River systems cutting east and west from this spine, creating the valleys where most people live. Forests covering most of the lowlands — dense, difficult to traverse, full of life. Open uplands on the higher ground — windswept, treeless, with long views.

**Fixed landmarks across every generation:** the four edges and their approximate positions; the mountainous spine; a great chalk escarpment in the south, running east to west, visible for miles; the major estuary in the west; the great sandbank island in the water-lands; the overall scale. Two players comparing experiences would recognise the same world — "yes, the ice in the north, the water-lands in the east, the chalk in the south" — but have completely different specific landscapes, people, and stories.

## Geology

The rock determines the soil, the soil determines the trees, the trees determine what lives there, and what lives there determines what the people know and believe. Seven geological zones produce the game's regional distinctiveness, each creating different food sources, building materials, craft possibilities, sacred place types, travel experiences, and human cultures.

**Chalk** dominates the south. Soft white rock that erodes into rolling downland — open, grassy, treeless on the hilltops. Thin alkaline soil. Where the chalk is cut, it's white — visible for miles. Chalk is porous, so rivers disappear underground and resurface as springs. Springs are sacred — they're where water emerges from the body of the earth. Flint occurs naturally in chalk, so this is where the best tool-making stone is found. The barrows along the chalk escarpment are the most visible monuments in the game — white mounds on a white ridge, seen from a day's walk away.

**Limestone** is similar to chalk but harder, older, more fractured. Thin, well-drained soil, sparse trees, exposed grey rock in pavements and outcrops. Limestone dissolves. Water carves through it. This is where caves happen — gorges, sinkholes, underground rivers, cave systems ranging from shallow overhangs to deep networks. Limestone also produces distinctive fertile valleys — green, sheltered, good for settlement.

**Sandstone** is softer, warm-coloured — red, orange, brown. Erodes into escarpments, overhangs, and shallow caves. Easy to carve, which means it's where you find the most marks, the most symbols. A sandstone outcrop with a sheltered face is a natural place for someone to scratch a record. The soil is sandy, acidic, supporting heathland and pine rather than rich deciduous woodland.

**Granite** forms the far west and the mountain spine. Hard, ancient, resistant. Tors, boulder fields, bare exposed summits. Soil is thin and acid where it exists at all. Moorland, bog, tough grasses. Harsh country for settlement but powerful country for sacred places — granite tors look like monuments even when they're natural, and it's not always clear which stones were placed by people and which by geology.

**Slate and shale** make up the western mountains and coastal areas. Thin, layered rock that splits cleanly. Steep, wet, with fast rivers running through narrow valleys. Dense oak woodland in the valleys, bare rock above. Settlements are in the valleys, connected to each other by river rather than by ridgeway. Travel means following water.

**Clay and alluvium** fill the lowland river valleys, particularly in the east. Heavy, waterlogged, immensely fertile. This is where the thickest forests grow — dense, heavy, oak and elm, almost impenetrable in places. The clay lowlands are where the most people can live, because the soil produces the most food, but the forest makes travel difficult. Settlements are in clearings, connected by river. This is also where the transition to the water-lands begins — the clay gets wetter as you move east until the ground becomes marsh, then reed bed, then open water.

**Glacial debris** defines the north. Not a rock type but a geological condition — the landscape left behind by retreating ice. Moraines of jumbled rock and gravel. Erratic boulders dropped by glaciers, sitting incongruously on the surface. Meltwater channels. No soil to speak of, just raw mineral rubble slowly being colonised by moss and lichen. No trees yet, or only the first pioneer birch and willow.

Each geological zone produces different food, different craft materials, different sacred places, different travel, and different people. Crossing from one zone to another, the human world shifts too. The generation algorithm places these zones broadly following the fixed frame — chalk in the south, granite and slate in the west, clay in the east, glacial debris in the north — but with the messy overlaps and intrusions of real geology.

## The Material World

The people of The Barrow do not have iron. Bronze is extraordinarily rare — most people have never seen it. Copper is seen occasionally, but it's soft. This constrains and defines the entire material culture.

**Flint is king.** The hardest, sharpest material available for cutting and shaping everything else. It comes from chalk, which means the southern chalk regions sit on the most strategically important mineral resource in the world. A person who can knap good flint is valuable anywhere. A person who can knap excellent flint — fine blades, precise arrowheads — is a person of real status, because the difference between crude and beautiful is hundreds of hours of accumulated practice. Flint doesn't occur everywhere. A player who travels north into granite country is carrying their flint with them, and when it breaks, they can't easily replace it.

**Wood is the universal material.** Houses, boats, tools, handles, containers, trackways, fences, fish traps. Woodworking without metal is a specific discipline — splitting along the grain with antler wedges, shaping by burning and scraping, joining with pegs and bindings and clever joinery. Different trees serve different purposes: ash for tool handles, oak for building, willow for weaving, yew for bows, birch bark for waterproof containers. Knowing which tree does what is practical craft knowledge with real consequences.

**Bone and antler are precision tools.** Antler pressure-flakers for fine knapping. Bone needles for sewing. Bone awls for piercing. Antler picks for digging. These materials come from animals and are not unlimited. A good set of antler tools is worth carrying carefully.

**Pottery changes storage and cooking.** Heavy but transformative. Fragile — a broken pot is a real loss. The style of pottery marks identity, because different communities make different shapes with different decorations. A pot is as readable as a tattoo.

**Copper is rare, soft, and beautiful.** Hammered from native copper found in rock into beads, pins, small blades that bend too easily for real work but gleam like nothing else in this world. Copper signals connection to trade networks.

**Bronze.** The men from across the water with bronze at their throats carry something that most people in this world have never seen. Harder than copper, holds an edge, worked by people who possess knowledge — tin and copper alloyed, smelted, cast — that is genuinely arcane. A bronze blade is not just a better tool. It's evidence of a knowledge system most of the world doesn't have access to.

**Tin** exists in stream gravels in the far western granite peninsula. Nobody in this world has connected it to copper. The men from across the water have. A player who accumulates enough metallurgical knowledge from different sources could theoretically discover bronze-making — an enormous late-game possibility that the game never announces.

**Colours and pigments.** Red ochre from iron-rich clay — the first pigment, used for tens of thousands of years. Yellow ochre. White from chalk. Black from charcoal. In a world where everything is brown, grey, green, a streak of vivid red on someone's face is startling. Rarer pigments exist — a blue-grey clay found in one valley, a copper-green patina. The men from across the water might have a purple dye from a sea creature nobody on the mainland has ever seen.

**Minerals and crystals.** Quartz in granite country — milky, sometimes clear, extraordinary in a world without glass. A clear quartz crystal would be sacred. Jet from the coast — black, polishable, light enough to wear. And a banded mineral, purple and gold, found only in one cave system in one limestone region. Anyone wearing it has been there. It's a geographical signature.

**The impossible objects.** No more than five in the entire world. Most players will never see any of them; they may only hear of them in word-tellings. A blade of silvery metal — meteoric iron, harder than flint, harder than anything, cold to the touch, inexplicable. It would be kept in the most sacred place by the most trusted guardian, and seeing it would require the deepest trust in the game. Perhaps a stone that's warm when it should be cold. A piece of amber with something inside it that resembles no living creature. A fragment of pottery harder than any local pot, decorated with unknown symbols, found in a sealed barrow chamber. These objects are evidence of the deep layer — The Old Knowledge — manifesting as physical things. Scarcity creates meaning. If there are five in the world, each one is an event. And possessing one changes the player's identity in ways that aren't entirely positive.

## Language

Language in The Barrow operates in three layers, generated alongside geology as one of the two deepest systems in the world.

**The living languages** form a continuous, shifting gradient across the landscape, shaped by geography. Ease of sustained contact determines linguistic similarity. People who can easily visit each other regularly speak more alike. People separated by mountains drift apart. River valleys create linguistic corridors; watersheds create linguistic boundaries. Three or four root language families are placed broadly across the mainland, following the major geographical divisions, but the boundaries are gradients, not lines. Two valleys geographically close but separated by a ridge might speak recognisably different branches of the same root. Two settlements a hundred miles apart but connected by a coastal trade route might understand each other well.

For the player, this means the texture of communication changes as they travel. Time spent building shared language with Breca transfers partially to others in her region, because the underlying language is related. But it helps not at all with people from across a major linguistic boundary. Crossing a mountain pass, language might change abruptly. Walking along a river valley, it shifts gradually. The player learns to read these patterns, and reading them becomes a deep form of landscape knowledge.

**The Old Tongues** are the primary linguistic artefact of the deep past, woven into everything. Every time someone speaks the name of a river or a hill, they're using a word that's older than their own language. They don't know this, mostly. It's just what the river is called.

The Old Tongues name everything: places, seasons (especially where the lunar and solar cycles converge), hills, rivers, springs, landscapes — escarpments, ranges, coastlines. They name things that were here but are now gone — glaciers, volcanoes. They name events that live on in memory — tsunamis, earthquakes, eruptions, great influxes of water, storms that reshaped the coast. They name gods. And they name legendary people — figures in the word-tellings who had god-adjacent powers, who travelled impossible distances, who had great strength or incredible wisdom, who communed with gods, who spoke to the dead, who vanished into caves and reappeared years later unchanged.

The same root appears in the name of a spring in the south and a lake in the north. A word for a hill sounds like a word for a god. The name of a valley, parsed differently, describes an event. A legendary woman's name contains a root the player has heard in the name of a cave, a sacred pool, a word for a particular kind of seeing. The names aren't labels. They're compressed stories. The meaning is recoverable if the player gathers enough fragments.

The generation system needs a set of perhaps fifty to a hundred Old Tongue roots with meanings, and rules for how they combine. From that, the algorithm produces names for everything in the landscape that are both euphonic and semantically connected. The player hears hundreds of names. Most wash over them. But the ones that rhyme with each other across distance — those are the threads that lead to Pattern and The Old Knowledge.

The legendary figures named in the Old Tongues serve a specific function: they're precedents for what the player is doing. The woman who walked into a cave and emerged on the coast is a word-telling about Passage. The man who spoke to gods is a word-telling about The Old Knowledge. The player is retracing paths the legendary figures walked. Their names, when decoded, are clues — not directions, but resonances, confirmations.

**The arriving tongue** is genuinely foreign. The men from across the water speak a language from a completely different family. There is no creole. No gradual mixing has occurred — these people have arrived too recently. Their language is mutually unintelligible with anything the native people speak.

A mere handful of native people can translate, and they do so imperfectly. These translators are among the most important individuals in the world. Each occupies an extraordinary social position — perhaps a child of a mixed union, a foster-child, or someone of unusual linguistic ability who attached themselves to the arrivals. They're valued by both sides but fully trusted by neither, because they can say things in one language that the other side can't verify. They have their own agendas. What they choose to translate and what they choose to omit is invisible to everyone else.

The player's access to anything the arriving people know — their word-tellings, their knowledge of bronze, their gods — is filtered through one of perhaps three or four translators in the entire world. The translation is imperfect. The player receives knowledge through two layers of imperfect transmission — the speaker's telling, filtered through the translator's understanding, delivered in a shared language the player only partially commands. Multiple versions of the same story, received through different translators, triangulate toward truth. Something is always lost. Something might be added.

This makes the arriving people permanently somewhat mysterious. Their bronze, their language, their gods point to a place the player has never been and probably never will go. That horizon beyond the southern sea is one of the game's edges, experienced through people rather than geography.

## Caves and the Underground

Caves are the game's most potent spaces, where every system converges. The central metaphor is crawling through a dark passage. The deepest knowledge is underground. The dead are buried in chambers. The oldest marks are on cave walls. And the physical experience of going underground — light vanishing, sound changing, temperature shifting, the weight of rock above — is unlike anything else in the game.

**Rock shelters and overhangs** are the shallowest. Not really caves — just places where rock provides a roof. The most common underground spaces, serving a practical function first: shelter. Some show signs of long use — soot-blackened ceilings, worn floors, the bones of old meals. Some have marks at the entrance, where the light is good. Simple marks at first. Later, more deliberate. Symbols.

**Shallow caves** extend beyond the reach of daylight. The player needs fire to proceed — a torch, which burns and eventually goes out. Light is finite. You can only go as deep as your light allows, and then you must turn back. Preparation is a form of knowledge. Shallow caves might branch once or twice. The text changes in character underground — shorter sentences, more attention to sound and touch. Some shallow caves are or were inhabited. A hermit. Someone hiding. Meeting someone in a cave is a different kind of encounter from meeting someone in a settlement.

**Deep cave systems** occur in limestone, where water has carved networks over geological time. Passages that branch and reconnect. Chambers that open suddenly — vast, echoing, the torchlight not reaching the far wall. Underground rivers. Pools of still black water. Formations that in torchlight look like they were made rather than grown.

A deep cave system is a multi-session exploration. The player can't map it in one visit. Over several visits they build a mental map, and the game never provides a drawn one. The cave exists in memory and attention.

In the deepest chambers — the ones that require real commitment to reach — the game places its most powerful underground content. Paintings on walls, marks older than anything on the surface, deposits of rare minerals, evidence of use ancient beyond reckoning. And in the deepest places, things that are genuinely strange: a natural formation that looks shaped by hands, a chamber where the acoustics produce sounds like voices, a passage that opens into a space that feels too regular, too deliberate, too meant. The game never confirms whether these experiences are natural or made.

**Sound** is how Body Sense works underground. Dripping water, echoes, the way breathing sounds when walls are close. In large chambers, the echo changes and the player senses the space before their torch reveals it. Wind through a narrow passage, water falling distantly, resonances that could be natural or could be something else.

**Air** is both information and danger. Moving air tells you there's another opening. Still, stale air is a warning. Bad air — low oxygen — makes the player lightheaded.

**Water** underground is both obstacle and guide. A river flowing through a cave system is going somewhere. Pools might be shallow or bottomless. Water in caves is black and reflects torchlight strangely.

### Ritual Darkness

The relationship between the player and light underground transforms over the course of the game. Early on, light is a constraint to manage — the most important resource underground. The game teaches, through lived experience, that darkness underground is the most vulnerable state possible.

But the deepest knowledge belongs to those who enter the dark without light. The word-tellings speak of legendary figures who "followed the god" through the cave — and every listener assumes divine guidance, supernatural sight. In truth they were using touch and memory. The absence of light during the passage is intrinsically important.

Late in the game, the player may reach a point where the next step requires them to choose vulnerability. To put out the torch. To enter a passage in total darkness. Not because their light ran out, but because the ritual demands it.

The game's text changes completely. No visual description. Only touch — the wall under the hand, the floor under the feet, the shape of the passage as it narrows and turns. Sound — breathing, water, echo. And memory — the player has been in this cave before with light. They know, roughly, what's here. But knowing it in the light and knowing it in the dark are different kinds of knowledge. A player who has visited this cave multiple times, who has built a mental map through repetition and attention, can navigate in darkness because they've earned the knowledge. A player who rushes in cannot.

And then the ritual lighting of the flame at the deepest point. The player has been in darkness for an unknowable time. Time dissolves without light. They've been following the wall, counting turnings, trusting memory and hands. They feel the space open. The echo changes. They light the flame.

And what they see in that first moment of firelight — after the dark, after the passage, after the fear — lands with a force that no graphical spectacle could match. Whatever is in that chamber is revealed in one sentence after a long silence.

The player who does this understands, afterwards, what the word-tellings truly mean. The legendary figure who "followed the god" through the cave knew the cave so well, had such complete trust in their own body's knowledge of the stone, that they could walk through absolute darkness and arrive exactly where they intended. The god they followed was their own accumulated knowledge. The word-telling, heard again after this experience, means something completely different.

The cave ritual is a rite of passage. The person who emerges is not who went in. And it prepares the player for Passage — the final threshold. The barrow is a constructed cave. The ritual of the barrow is the ritual of the cave formalised and concentrated. The player who has already surrendered light and trusted the dark is prepared, though not guaranteed safe passage.

## The Wights

The Barrow's world is warped in one crucial respect: other species of human persist here.

**The cave-wights** — known by whatever name the local people use — are neanderthals. Short, strong, intelligent, secretive, knowing the land better than any human ever could. Almost impossible to track. They've been in the limestone cave systems for longer than modern humans have been on this landmass. They know passages no human has found. They can navigate in darkness not through ritual but through generations of embodied knowledge — exactly the skill the cave ritual teaches the player, but taken to a level humans can only approach through spiritual practice.

Most players will experience the cave-wights only as the people of The Barrow experience them — as signs, as stories, as a feeling of being watched, as a trail that ends at a rock face. A hunter finds a trail that vanishes impossibly. A woman gathering herbs feels observed and finds nothing when she turns. Children are warned. The wise woman knows more, and doesn't share readily.

In the word-tellings, the cave-wights are spoken of with a mixture of fear and respect. They're powerful. They're old. Some tellings suggest they were here before the gods. Some suggest they are gods, or were once. The wise woman's knowledge of caves might include knowledge that came, generations back, from observation of cave-wight behaviour. What humans learned about the underground, they may have learned by watching beings who already knew.

The deepest chambers of cave systems contain paintings, marks, arrangements of bone — and some of these things might not have been made by humans at all.

A player who somehow, over a very long time, through patience and presence and the absolute suppression of threat, builds something approaching trust with a cave-wight has access to knowledge of the landscape that no human possesses. Where the deep caves connect. Where water flows underground. Where the oldest marks are. But this should be almost impossibly rare. Most players will never achieve it.

**The small folk** are another species — smaller and slighter than humans, fast, less intelligent in the human sense, rare, found only in the warmer, wetter places. They're in the reed beds, the warm springs, the dense wet woodland of the clay lowlands and the edges of the water-lands. More likely to be glimpsed than the cave-wights, but less comprehensible. Their behaviour looks random until watched long enough to see the pattern. They follow routes that might trace underground water courses or invisible geological features. They eat things humans don't recognise as food. They make sounds that aren't quite language.

In the word-tellings, the small folk are spoken of with wonder, unease, sometimes tenderness. They're seen as fragile, as remnants, as something fading. The warm wet places where they're found are treated as sanctuaries partly because the small folk are there. Disturbing those places is taboo not because of abstract sacredness but because something lives there that mustn't be driven away. The tellings about them have a quality of elegy — they were more numerous once, they're fewer now, someday they'll be gone.

Both species recontextualise the Body Sense node. That feeling of being watched in a cave, the wrongness in a wood at dusk — sometimes it's the thin membrane between worlds. Sometimes it's a wight. The game never tells you which. And it embodies the game's theme of deep time and declining knowledge in living form. The wights are not relics. They're alive, right now, carrying knowledge that predates everything the human world has built. And they're declining.

The game must never fully explain what these beings are. No bestiary. No moment of classification. The player's understanding is always partial, always filtered through observation and story.

## Movement and Choice

You move through this world the way a person moves through a landscape on foot. Your fundamental choices are small and human. Push through the wood or climb the hill above the treeline? The wood is slow going, but sheltered — there might be food, there might be people. The hill is cold and wind-scoured and empty, but you can see for miles, and the walking is faster. Stay another night by this river, or press on before the weather turns?

There is no combat system. There are dangers — things in the dark, weather that can kill you, people who might not welcome you — but your relationship to danger is the rogue's relationship: you read, you avoid, you route around, you flee. The tension is the tension of vulnerability in a large and indifferent landscape.

The game is played in short sessions. You walk, you make a few choices, you reach a natural resting place — a shelter, a settlement, a cairn on a hilltop — and you stop. Between sessions, time passes. The seasons turn. The moon shifts phase. The world moves without you. The game respects your absence rather than penalising it.

Weather isn't decoration — it's a force that shapes decisions. Rain pushes you to shelter, and sheltering means staying, and staying means noticing, and noticing means discovering. A storm isn't an inconvenience — it's the game saying be here for a while. Snow closes the mountain passes and your plans change entirely. The seasons reshape the possibility space cyclically, and winter reasserts survival as a primary concern no matter how much the player has learned.

## Tarrying

Most games treat standing still as wasted time. In The Barrow, attention is the primary currency.

Some places reveal nothing to someone passing through, but open slowly to someone who stays. A pool that changes at dusk. A sound in a ruin that resolves into something recognisable if you wait through the initial unease. A quality of light in a clearing that appears only when the rain stops at a particular time of day. Staying near a person over time builds trust even without language contact, unless stronger enmity exists.

The player learns when to move and when to stay. This is itself a kind of knowledge — perhaps the most important kind.

## People and Relationship

The landscape is populated, sparsely, by people.

An apothecary in a walled town. A wise woman who moves within her valley but rarely leaves it. Men from across the water, richly dressed, who travel to a particular stone circle at midsummer and then push north, following a route their forebears walked, according to the word-telling.

These people are not quest-givers. They are not vendors. They are carriers of knowledge, and their knowledge is partial, situated, and shaped by where they've been. They move or stay in plausible ways. The apothecary stays in her town. The wise woman moves within her valley. The men from across the water follow routes dictated by their word-tellings and the sacred calendar.

**Communication** starts simple with each new person. "She offers dried fish. She points north." Over visits, the conversation deepens. The depth of relationship determines the depth of knowledge available. The deepest knowledge can only come from someone who trusts you and with whom you've built enough shared understanding to hold a complex idea. The player never sees a vocabulary counter. They notice that this person tells them more now.

**Trust** is built through time, presence, and reciprocity, following what anthropologists like Dunbar describe. You come to the wise woman's valley. You come back. You bring something she needs, not because she asked but because you noticed. She tells you something small — a test. You return. You understand enough to ask the right next question. Trust is damaged by negative encounters and affected by identity markers. You can maintain real relationships with only a few people, because relationships take time and presence and you can't be everywhere.

**Reciprocity** in a pre-monetary society is gift economy. You bring herbs not to buy information but to weave yourself into a web of mutual obligation. She remembers. She tells you something next time. Maybe she tells someone else about you, and when you meet that person in a different part of the landscape, they already know your name.

**Reputation** isn't a number. It's a network of stories about you that propagate through the world at the speed of human travel.

## Identity

Your identity is not a character sheet. It's a life.

Every choice is legible to the people you meet. Your tattoos tell someone which people you've lived among. The food you eat tells them whether you're from the coast or the uplands. The name of the god you speak at a shrine tells anyone watching where your allegiances sit. The words in your mouth carry the cadences of the people you've spent the most time with. Your craft marks you — a fine arrowhead is known by its maker.

None of this is cosmetic. Your visible history opens and closes doors constantly, most of which you can't see. These choices are partially irreversible. You can learn new ways, slowly. But you can't un-speak a god's name.

**The player begins without lineage.** In a culture where your genealogy is your credentials, having no people is not a neutral state. It's profoundly suspicious. You are nobody's child. This is an active problem, not just an absence.

Every playthrough produces a different person, not because the map is different, but because you are.

## The Quest

You are not given a quest.

In the early sessions, you are simply surviving, exploring, beginning to understand the local landscape and its people. You accumulate fragments — a story, a symbol seen in two places, a path that leads somewhere you can't yet reach.

The game scatters seeds. It watches which ones you water.

Somewhere in those early hours, something catches. A thread you want to pull. The game doesn't tell you this is your quest. There's no quest log, no map marker. Your quest is the thing you find yourself caring about, and the game's job is to ensure that when you pull that thread, it connects to other threads, in a web that leads deeper into the world.

The early game — the emergence period before the quest crystallises — needs the most carefully interwoven content. Not the most content, but the most richly interconnected. A smaller region where almost anything the player does reveals something that points at something else.

You can fail. Not by dying — though you can die — but by losing the thread, by never reaching the place, by arriving too late, by never understanding the thing you needed to understand. The fear isn't death. It's that you came all this way and didn't get there.

## The System Architecture

Beneath the surface, the game is a web of interlocking systems — a graph of capacities and knowledge, where one thing leads to another, like the resource web of an incremental game.

**Body (Tier 0).** Shelter, Food, and Body Sense — the animal knowledge beneath thought. The backs of your knees in a sacred place. Body Sense connects directly to Sacred Places, bypassing three tiers. You can feel a thin place on day one.

**Land (Tier 1).** Paths, Foraging, Weather Reading, Animal Signs, and Practice — embodied skill that improves through repetition.

**People (Tier 2).** Encounter, Shared Language, Trade, Local Lore, and The Near Dead — the recently dead, whose graves are tended, whose loss is present.

**Roots (Tier 3).** Trust (the bottleneck to everything deep), Craft (whose quality depends on Practice), Symbols, Reputation, Seasons (the solar year, learned by living through it), and Moon (the shorter monthly cycle — herbs gathered at different phases, tides, things visible only when full, things done only at the dark).

**Deep (Tier 4).** Word-Tellings (oral histories, each teller with their own version, truth triangulated from differences), Sacred Places (accessible from the start through Body Sense but understood only with everything else), Ritual (not worship but maintenance — the rites keep the world functioning), Pattern (where symbols, tellings, and sacred places converge), and The Far Dead (the ancient dead, the barrow-builders, whose bones are in the land and whose marks are on the stones).

**Threshold (Tier 5).** The Old Knowledge (what the land remembers, what the stones were placed to mark) and Passage (the barrow — transformation that requires sacrifice, the loss of something real).

Key structural features: Trust is the skeleton key, gated by time and presence, resistant to optimisation. Seasons presses downward onto Shelter and Food, reasserting survival cyclically. Reputation feeds back into Encounter — your name arrives before you do. Lore feeds back into Paths — stories reveal routes. Pattern feeds back into Symbols — understanding makes new marks visible. Ritual and Symbols are not directly connected; they converge at Pattern through Sacred Places and Word-Tellings. Presence (tarrying) deepens Trust, Shared Language, Seasons, Moon, Sacred Places, and Ritual.

## Interface

The game is text. Occasional illustrations in a spare, consistent style — line art reminiscent of early twentieth-century book illustration — appear at significant moments. Not every screen. Perhaps a few hundred curated drawings covering recurring elements: stone types, shelter types, landscape moments, artefacts, animal signs. The appearance of an illustration is itself a signal — the game saying look at this.

The typography is a serif face — something that feels like a printed page, an old book found in a second-hand shop that turns out to be extraordinary.

The player interacts through contextual, sparse prompts that emerge from the situation. Not typed commands (parser friction kills atmosphere), not pure multiple choice (reveals too much, collapses possibility). Instead, a small number of actions phrased as natural impulses, whose number and nature change depending on the situation. Three options when walking a ridge. Four when arriving at a settlement where you know people. Two when facing a stranger in a cave. The game never tells the player which choice matters most.

When navigating landscape, the choices concern where to put your body. When among people, the choices concern relationship — who to spend time with, what to give, whether to stay or go. The options available expand as the player accumulates knowledge — a choice that only appears because you learned something three weeks ago from someone in a different valley.

A single clean screen. Text above, options below. No map, no inventory panel, no stats sidebar. Perhaps a subtle indicator of season and time of day. Perhaps nothing except the words.

## The Three Layers

The game's text generation operates on three layers.

**The graph layer** is the world model — the most stable layer. It knows what exists, where, and how things connect. Breca's settlement by the river. The stone circle three days north. The spiral symbol's connections to four locations and two people. This layer changes rarely during play.

**The state layer** tracks everything in motion. Player location, time, season, moon phase, what they carry, who they've met, relationship depths, what they've been told, what they've seen, whether they're cold, whether it's raining. This layer changes with every choice.

**The voice layer** produces the text the player reads. Descriptive atmosphere — landscape, weather, sensory detail — is drawn from a hand-authored fragment library, curated for the game's specific literary voice: short sentences, concrete detail, no purple prose, trust the reader. Relational and narrative content — what people tell you, how encounters unfold — is generated by a language model given tight constraints: current state, voice guidelines, and what it must and must not say. The fragment library is the skeleton. The model fills in the living tissue. Everything is filtered through the voice layer that enforces tone.

The same state must produce different text each time — not wildly different, but varied enough that the player never feels they're reading a template. The voice of the game is everything. "Pig tracks in the mud. The smell of smoke is stronger." That precision is what the fragment library provides. The LLM is a limited and controlled contributor, not a core feature.

## Lunar and Solar Time

Two temporal cycles shape the game.

**The solar year** turns slowly. Seasons change food sources, travel conditions, settlement patterns, and the gatherings that bring people together. The first winter is a crisis — the player doesn't know where to find food in the cold months, doesn't know which shelters hold. If alone, they might not survive without preparation. If with people, they survive because of Trust. The second winter is different — the player knows things now. The survival pressure is the same but their capacity to meet it has transformed.

**The lunar month** turns faster, providing a short pulse within the long seasonal rhythm. New moon, waxing, full, waning. The wise woman gathers herbs at specific phases. Tides change near the coast and in the water-lands, governing access to paths and places. Things reveal themselves at full moon that are hidden otherwise. Things are done at the dark of the moon that cannot be done in moonlight. Stone circles may be aligned to lunar risings at the extremes of an eighteen-year cycle.

For a game of short sessions, the lunar cycle ensures that the world is always slightly different when the player returns.

## What The Barrow Is

The Barrow is a game about a person walking through a landscape, accumulating knowledge that nobody else has in quite the same combination, gradually becoming the only person who can do a particular thing.

The primary emotion is quiet anticipation. Not adrenaline, not triumph, not anxiety. The gentle pull of "I wonder what happens if..." and "I think I'm getting close to something."

It's like being out for a long walk and enjoying the surroundings but also wondering what's over the next hill.

The landscape is ancient Britain and not-Britain. The version that still exists in the imagination — accessible, as it always was, by crawling into a barrow and out the other side. The land before the Romans, thick with paganism, mystery, lore, and rites. Stone circles and standing stones. Ley lines and ancient trackways. Celtic barrows and derelict towers. Hidden tombs and cave systems. Isolated lochans high in the hills. Mountain passes and windswept valleys. Ruins whose purpose has been forgotten by everyone except the land itself.

The magic is not fireballs and mana pools. It's the older thing. Runes, ley lines, wise women, hidden doors that open when you know the words. The sense that the membrane between this world and something else is thin in certain places, at certain times, and that the people who knew how to navigate that are mostly gone — but their knowledge is still here, embedded in the stones and the paths and the names of places, waiting for someone to learn how to read it.

Other beings share this landscape. They were here first. They know things humans have forgotten or never knew. They are declining, and the world will be diminished when they're gone.

The game is called The Barrow because that's how you get there, and that's what it feels like to play: crawling through a dark, narrow passage, not knowing what's on the other side, but trusting that the passage goes somewhere.

A barrow is a grave. The player crawls through the place of the dead. What comes out is not who went in.
