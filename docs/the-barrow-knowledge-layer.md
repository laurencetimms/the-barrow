# The Barrow — The Knowledge Layer

## The web of meaning: common knowledge, deep knowledge, symbols, word-tellings, the Old Tongue, and the threads that connect them.

---

## 1. Overview

The knowledge layer weaves meaning through landscape and habitation. It sits above both and below the relationship layer. It is the most complex layer in the game and the one most responsible for The Barrow's distinctive experience — the sense that the world contains discoverable depth, that things connect across distances, and that understanding is always partial but growing.

The knowledge layer has two strata: **common knowledge** (the shared fabric of understanding that the player absorbs through proximity and time) and **deep knowledge** (the web of specific connections, hidden meanings, and gated secrets that the player discovers through effort, trust, and attention). Common knowledge is the grammar. Deep knowledge is the poetry. You need the grammar to read the poetry.

The knowledge layer also defines the **convergence conditions** that determine when the voice layer is permitted to push toward the liminal — the supernatural-as-convergence principle documented in the System Layers document.

---

## 2. Common Knowledge

Common knowledge is the vast body of understanding shared freely, learned through proximity and time, and forming the foundation that makes deep knowledge meaningful. It is documented in outline in the System Layers document (Section 3.1-3.2). This section expands on how it's generated, distributed, and acquired.

### 2.1 The Four Registers

**Universal knowledge.** Shared by virtually everyone regardless of region or culture. The sun's behaviour, fire, seasons, basic survival, the reality of death, the existence of the dead in barrows. Acquired almost immediately through any human contact. Represented as a baseline set of knowledge tags applied to the player state after the first few encounters.

**Regional common knowledge.** Specific to a geographic area. Local geography, local food sources, local hazards, local paths, local names for features, the local name for the cave-wights. Acquired through any encounter in the region, even casual ones. Resets when the player enters a new region — they must learn the local knowledge again. Represented as location-tagged knowledge tags.

**Cultural common knowledge.** Shared by a cultural group — beliefs, practices, calendar observances, basic mythology, attitudes toward the sacred, attitudes toward the arriving people. Requires some shared language to acquire, because it concerns abstract concepts. Differs between cultures, and the differences are informative. Represented as culture-tagged knowledge tags.

**Specialist common knowledge.** Shared within a community of practice — healers, flint-knappers, boat-builders, fishers, animal-tenders. Acquired through proximity to practitioners and shared activity. Represented as practice-tagged knowledge tags.

### 2.2 Generation of Common Knowledge

Common knowledge is not individually authored. It's generated from templates based on the cultural group, regional context, and geological zone.

Each cultural group has a **common knowledge template** that defines:
- Which gods are honoured and in what manner.
- What the major calendar observances are and what they signify.
- What the cultural attitudes toward the dead, the sacred, the cave-wights, the small folk, and the arriving people are.
- What the basic mythological framework is — how the world was made, why the seasons turn, what happens after death.
- What the major taboos are — what you don't do, where you don't go, what you don't say.

Each region has a **regional knowledge template** that defines:
- Local names for landscape features (drawn from the Old Tongue naming system and the current language variety).
- Local food sources by season.
- Local paths and their conditions.
- Local hazards — flooding, unstable ground, dangerous animals, places to avoid.
- Local names and attitudes toward wight presences if any exist in the region.

Each specialist role has a **practice knowledge template** that defines:
- What the practitioner knows about their craft materials and techniques.
- Where the best materials are found.
- Seasonal patterns relevant to the practice.
- Connections to other practitioners — who the best flint-knapper is, where the finest pottery is made.

These templates are filled with specifics during generation — regional names from the language system, local resources from the geology, cultural details from the cultural group's generated properties. The result is a body of common knowledge that feels specific and local even though it's produced from systematic templates.

### 2.3 Acquisition by the Player

Common knowledge is absorbed, not delivered. The player acquires it through encounters and time, without specific trust requirements (beyond basic communication for cultural knowledge). The voice layer manages the transition — early encounters in a new region include more contextual information, presented as things the person is telling or showing the player. Later encounters assume the player already knows, because they do.

The pacing of acquisition matters. Universal knowledge arrives in the first session. Regional knowledge accumulates over the first few sessions in a new area. Cultural knowledge builds over weeks of contact with a community. Specialist knowledge requires sustained proximity to a practitioner — helping, watching, participating. None of this is instant, and none of it is gated by dialogue choices. It accrues through presence.

## 3. Deep Knowledge: Structure

Deep knowledge is the web of specific connections, hidden meanings, and gated secrets that forms the game's primary discovery system. Unlike common knowledge, which is a field the player moves through and absorbs, deep knowledge is a **graph** — a network of nodes connected by edges, where each node is a specific piece of meaning and each edge is a relationship between pieces.

### 3.1 What Deep Knowledge Contains

The deep knowledge graph consists of:

**Symbol instances.** Specific marks at specific sites — the spiral on Breca's wicker panel, the same spiral on the cairn, the same spiral on the bronze amulet. Each instance is a node. The edges connect instances of the same symbol across different locations and contexts.

**Word-telling instances.** Specific tellings by specific people — the version of the journey-north story that Breca's elder tells, the version the wise woman tells, the version accessible through the translator from the men across the water. Each instance is a node. Edges connect variants of the same core narrative.

**Old Tongue name instances.** Specific names attached to specific landscape features, gods, legendary figures, seasons, and events. Each name is a node. Edges connect names that share roots, and edges connect names to the things they name.

**Sacred site properties.** Specific significance attached to specific sites — astronomical alignment, ritual function, connection to word-tellings, connection to symbol systems. Each property is a node connected to the site and to related knowledge elsewhere.

**Ritual fragments.** Specific pieces of ritual knowledge — what to do, when, where, with what, and why. Each fragment is a node. Edges connect fragments that belong to the same ritual, and edges connect rituals to the sites, times, and conditions where they apply.

**Impossible object properties.** The specific nature, history, and significance of each impossible object. Each is a node connected to the word-tellings that reference it, the site where it's kept, and the deep knowledge web it participates in.

**Pattern connections.** The meta-level connections that link different knowledge types — a symbol that appears in a word-telling, a word-telling that references a sacred site, a sacred site whose name in the Old Tongue contains a root that connects to a legendary figure. Pattern connections are the highest-value edges in the graph, because they're where the player experiences the convergence of multiple knowledge streams into a single understanding.

### 3.2 Threads

A **thread** is a connected subgraph within the deep knowledge graph — a set of related nodes and edges that, when followed, leads the player through a sequence of discoveries toward a deeper understanding. Threads are the primary unit of player-driven narrative in The Barrow.

The spiral thread: the player sees a mark on Breca's panel. Later, they see the same mark on a cairn. Later still, they see it on a bronze amulet. Each sighting is a node. The edges connect them. Following the thread means travelling to the places where the spiral appears, talking to the people who know about it, hearing the word-tellings that reference it, and eventually understanding what the spiral means within the Old Tongue naming system and the sacred framework.

A thread has no prescribed order. The player can encounter its nodes in any sequence, depending on where they go and who they talk to. The thread doesn't have a "start" — any node can be the entry point. And the thread doesn't have a definitive "end" — understanding deepens incrementally, with no single moment of complete revelation. There is always another connection to discover, another variant to hear, another site to visit.

Threads can intersect. The spiral thread might share nodes with the journey-north thread, if the word-telling about the journey mentions the spiral. The player following one thread encounters connections to another, and the web of meaning expands. The game's deepest rewards come from following multiple threads and discovering where they converge — which is the Pattern node in the system graph.

### 3.3 Thread Density and Distribution

The deep knowledge graph must be dense enough that every player finds threads to pull, regardless of where they start or which direction they travel. But it must also be sparse enough that discoveries feel earned rather than inevitable.

The generation distributes threads according to several principles:

**Geographic spread.** Each thread spans multiple districts and ideally multiple regions. The spiral is not confined to Breca's valley — it appears in the chalk south, in the limestone dale, and on the bronze carried from across the water. Following a thread requires travel.

**Multiple access points.** Each thread has several entry nodes distributed across the landscape. The player can encounter the spiral first on the cairn, first on the wicker panel, or first on the standing stone, depending on where they go. No thread has a single, missable entry point.

**Variable depth.** Some threads are shallow — three or four nodes connecting a local symbol to a local word-telling to a local sacred site. These are discoverable within a single region and provide the early game's sense of rewarded curiosity. Other threads are deep — a dozen or more nodes spanning the entire game world, connecting multiple symbol systems, multiple word-telling traditions, and multiple sacred sites. These are the game's long-term discovery arcs, sustaining curiosity across months of play.

**Interconnection.** Threads share nodes. The stone circle that belongs to the spiral thread also belongs to the midsummer-gathering thread and the men-from-across-the-water thread. The wise woman who carries a word-telling about the spiral also carries knowledge about the cave ritual. Interconnection means that following any thread leads to other threads, creating the web structure that makes the knowledge layer feel like a coherent whole rather than a set of isolated puzzles.

**The emergence region is denser.** The area where the player starts has more thread entry points per district than the wider world, ensuring that early play surfaces enough fragments to provoke curiosity. This density should feel natural — the emergence region is a well-inhabited area with active connections to the wider world — not like a tutorial zone.

### 3.4 How Threads Are Generated

Thread generation is one of the most important processes in the game's creation of a new world. It happens after the landscape and habitation layers are generated, because threads must reference specific sites, specific people, and specific landscape features.

The generation process:

**Define core narratives.** The game has a set of core narrative templates — perhaps ten to fifteen — that describe the deep stories of the world. The journey-north narrative. The woman-who-entered-the-cave narrative. The lost-land narrative (the water-lands). The forging narrative (bronze). The sacrifice narrative. The passage narrative. Each template has a structure — key events, key symbols, key places — but the specifics are filled during generation.

**Anchor narratives to landscape.** Each core narrative is connected to specific generated features. The journey-north narrative follows a specific route from the south coast to the ice margin, passing through specific stone circles and along specific trackways. The lost-land narrative references specific drowned settlements in the water-lands. The anchoring process ensures that every narrative is physically situated — it references real places the player can visit.

**Distribute narrative fragments.** Each core narrative is broken into fragments — word-telling variants, symbol instances, sacred site properties, Old Tongue name connections — and distributed across the people and places of the game world. The elder in Breca's settlement carries one fragment. The wise woman carries another. A mark on a cairn is a third. The men from across the water carry a variant through the translator. Distribution ensures that no single source contains the whole story, and that the player must gather fragments from multiple sources to build understanding.

**Create symbol systems.** Each core narrative has associated symbols — visual marks that appear at sites connected to the narrative. A symbol system consists of a primary symbol (the spiral, for instance) and possibly secondary symbols that relate to it. Symbol instances are placed at sites referenced by the narrative, but also at a few sites not directly referenced — creating the possibility of unexpected connections and the sense that the symbol system is older and larger than any single narrative.

**Connect narratives.** Where core narratives share themes, locations, or symbols, their fragments are allowed to overlap — the same site participates in multiple narratives, the same symbol appears in multiple contexts, the same word-telling references elements of more than one core narrative. This interconnection is what creates the web structure.

**Plant entry points.** For each thread, the generation places at least three to five easily accessible entry points — symbol instances at visible sites, word-telling fragments carried by individuals the player is likely to meet early, sacred site properties that are perceptible at moderate knowledge depth. The player should encounter fragments of multiple threads within their first season of play.

## 4. Symbols

### 4.1 What Symbols Are

Symbols are visual marks — carved, scratched, painted, or formed — that appear at sites across the landscape and on portable objects. They are the most immediately visible expression of the deep knowledge layer, because they can be seen before they can be understood.

A symbol is not a letter or a word. It's not a writing system. It's a condensed visual expression of a meaning that is more fully articulated in word-tellings, in ritual, and in the sacred framework. A spiral doesn't mean "journey" the way a word means something. It *participates* in a web of meaning connected to journeys, to cycles, to water, to the passage between states — and different cultures and different individuals emphasise different aspects of that web.

### 4.2 Symbol Types

**Cup marks.** Small, circular depressions pecked into stone. The simplest and most common marks in the game. Found across the entire landscape, on standing stones, on outcrops, on the entrance stones of barrows. Cup marks are old — older than the living cultures — and their original meaning may be partially or wholly lost. They are the baseline of the symbol system, the marks that everyone recognises but nobody fully understands.

**Spirals.** Curving lines that wind inward or outward. More complex than cup marks and less common. Found on standing stones, on the kerb stones of barrows, on cave walls, and on portable objects. Spirals have strong narrative connections — they appear in word-tellings about journeys, about cycles, about passage.

**Concentric circles.** Rings within rings, sometimes with a line extending outward from the centre. Related to spirals but distinct — where spirals suggest movement, concentric circles suggest containment, focus, a centre. Found at sacred sites, on standing stones, at cave entrances.

**Linear marks.** Lines, chevrons, zigzags, parallel grooves. More geometric than spirals or circles. Found on sandstone (easy to carve) and on portable objects. Linear marks may be part of a counting or recording system — tallies, seasonal markers, astronomical records.

**Figurative marks.** Rare. Representations of things — animals, human figures, objects. Found primarily in deep cave chambers and on portable objects. Figurative marks are the most narratively rich symbols, because they depict recognisable subjects, but their meaning is no more transparent than abstract marks. A deer carved on a cave wall might refer to a specific hunt, a specific god, a specific word-telling, or something else entirely.

**Composite marks.** Combinations of the above — a spiral surrounded by cup marks, a concentric circle with linear rays, a figurative element incorporated into an abstract pattern. Composite marks are the most complex and the most semantically dense. They appear at the most significant sites and on the most significant objects.

### 4.3 Symbol Systems

Symbols are organised into systems — sets of related marks that participate in the same web of meaning. The game generates three to five major symbol systems, each associated with one or more core narratives and distributed across the landscape.

Each symbol system has:

**A primary symbol.** The most recognisable mark — the spiral, the concentric circles, the branching line. This appears at many sites and is the most likely entry point for the player.

**Secondary symbols.** Related marks that elaborate or modify the primary symbol's meaning. A spiral with cup marks around it. A concentric circle with a specific number of rings. These appear at fewer sites and represent deeper knowledge.

**Contextual variation.** The same symbol looks different depending on the medium and the maker. A spiral scratched hastily into a wicker panel looks different from a spiral carefully pecked into a standing stone over many hours. The player learns to recognise the same symbol across different expressions — which is itself a form of knowledge.

**Geographic distribution.** Each symbol system has a geographic heartland — the region where it appears most densely — and a periphery where it appears more sparsely. The heartland is where the symbol's meaning is most fully understood by the local people. At the periphery, the symbol is recognised but its meaning is partial or different. This geographic variation means that the player's understanding of a symbol deepens as they travel toward its heartland.

**Temporal depth.** Some symbol instances are ancient — weathered, lichen-covered, part of constructions that predate living memory. Some are recent — freshly carved, clearly made by someone alive now. The same symbol persists across deep time, which tells the player that whatever it means, it has mattered for a very long time.

### 4.4 How the Player Encounters Symbols

The player sees symbols before understanding them. A mark on a rock face is just a mark until the player has accumulated enough knowledge — from other symbol sightings, from word-tellings, from cultural common knowledge — to recognise it as meaningful.

The knowledge-gating works through the player state. At low knowledge, the voice layer describes the mark physically: "Scratches on the stone, worn smooth. They might be deliberate or might be natural." At moderate knowledge, the player recognises pattern: "A mark on the stone — a spiral, similar to the one you saw on the cairn above Breca's valley." At high knowledge, the player reads meaning: "The spiral, but with three cup marks at its centre — a variant you haven't seen before. The wise woman spoke of the three wells. This might be connected."

The transition from seeing to recognising to understanding is one of the game's core pleasures. It happens gradually, through accumulation, and it transforms the landscape — places the player walked through blindly a month ago now speak to them because they've learned to read the marks.

## 5. Movable Symbols

### 5.1 Why Movable Symbols Matter

Fixed symbols — marks carved into standing stones, scratched on cave walls, pecked into outcrops — are the backbone of the symbol system. But in the real pre-literate world, symbolic objects move. They're made in one place, carried to another, traded, gifted, deposited as offerings, buried with the dead, lost, found. A carved figure made by someone in the chalk south might end up in a sacred pool in the granite west, carried there by a trader or a pilgrim or a person making an offering on behalf of a distant community.

That movement is itself information. It tells you about connections between places and people that the fixed landscape can't reveal. A figure carved from chalk-country flint, found in a granite-west sacred pool, is evidence of a relationship between those two regions. The material tells you where it was made. The location tells you where it ended up. The gap between the two is a story — a trade route, a pilgrimage, a gift, a theft.

Movable symbols add a dimension to the knowledge layer that fixed symbols can't provide: they carry geographic history in their material, and their current position tells you something about the human connections that brought them there.

### 5.2 Types of Movable Symbolic Objects

**Small carved figures.** Stone, bone, antler, or wood. Human forms, animal forms, abstract forms. Some are clearly representational — a deer, a woman, a face. Some are ambiguous — a shape that might be a body or might be something else. They vary enormously in quality, from crude scratchings to pieces of real artistry. A figure carved with a specific symbol connects to that symbol system.

**Marked stones.** Small, portable stones — river pebbles, flint nodules, pieces of quartz — with marks carved or scratched onto them. Simpler than figures but carrying the same symbolic weight. A pebble with a spiral is a portable instance of the spiral symbol system. These are the most common movable symbols.

**Beads and pendants.** Objects made to be worn — jet beads, amber pieces, bone pendants, shells with holes drilled through them. Some are purely ornamental. Some carry marks. Some are made from materials that are geographically specific — jet from the coast, amber from the east — so wearing one tells people where you've been or who you know. A pendant is a piece of identity worn on the body, readable to anyone who knows what the material and the marks signify.

**Deposits and offerings.** Objects that were movable and have been made fixed by the act of depositing them at a sacred site — placed in pools, in cave niches, at the base of standing stones, in the earth near barrows. Finding a carved figure in a sacred pool tells you that someone brought it here from wherever it was made, and the act of bringing it was meaningful. Over time, deposits accumulate at significant sacred sites — dozens of small carved figures, marked stones, and beads placed at the bases of stones, in niches, in the earth nearby. These accumulations are a record of devotion, evidence of how many people, over how long, have considered this place sacred. The quantity, variety, and geographic range of materials in the deposits tell the player about the site's importance and its connections to the wider world.

### 5.3 Movable Symbols and the Knowledge Graph

Movable symbolic objects are nodes in the deep knowledge graph, just like fixed symbol instances — but with a position property that can change. Each object has:

**Creation context.** Who made it (or what culture's tradition it belongs to), where it was made, and from what material. The material is the most durable piece of provenance — a figure carved from chalk flint is always from the chalk, regardless of where it's found.

**Current location.** Where the object is now — at a site, carried by a person, held by the player. The gap between creation context and current location is a story.

**Symbol-system connection.** Which marks the object bears and which symbol system they belong to. A marked stone with a spiral is a node in the spiral thread, connected to every other spiral instance in the graph.

**Condition.** How worn, how old, how well-preserved. A freshly carved figure and a figure worn smooth by centuries of handling tell different stories. Condition is visible in the voice layer's description and is a form of temporal information.

### 5.4 How the Player Encounters Movable Symbols

Movable symbols are uncommon but not rare. The player might encounter one every few sessions — found at a site, received as a gift, noticed in someone's possession, discovered in the debris of a settlement midden, or seen among the accumulated deposits at a sacred place.

**Finding.** A marked stone in a stream bed. A carved figure half-buried near a fire circle. A bead caught in the roots of a fallen tree. Found objects are fragments of someone else's story, disconnected from their context, waiting to be reconnected. The player who finds a carved bone figure on a forest path has found a question: who made this, who carried it, why is it here?

**Receiving.** The wise woman gives you a marked stone after your third visit. Breca shows you a carved figure that belonged to her mother. A trader offers a jet bead from the coast. Receiving a symbolic object is different from receiving food or materials — it carries meaning, and the act of giving it creates a bond. The wise woman didn't give you just any stone. She gave you *that* stone, with *that* mark, and the fact that she chose it means something.

**Observing.** The player notices a carved figure at the base of a standing stone — one of many deposits. They notice a pendant around someone's neck and recognise the mark on it. They see the accumulation of offerings at a sacred pool and understand, from the variety of materials, that people come here from many places. Observation of movable symbols in context is one of the ways the player reads the social and sacred geography of the world.

### 5.5 Carrying and Care

The player can carry movable symbolic objects. They're small — a handful at most can be carried without becoming a burden. The game should make it natural to have only a few at any time, because these are precious things, not inventory items. You'd regret losing one. You'd take care of it if you had one.

Carrying a marked object is an identity signal. When the player meets new people, those people might recognise the symbol — or not. A spiral carved on a stone you carry might open a conversation with someone who knows the spiral tradition, or it might provoke unease in someone for whom that symbol has different connotations. The player can choose to show an object, keep it hidden, or present it at a significant moment. Showing the right symbol to the right person at the right time might be the key that opens a conversation that wouldn't otherwise happen.

The player can also choose to **let go** of a symbolic object — and this should feel as meaningful as acquiring it. Leaving a marked stone at a sacred site is an act of offering, of participation in the tradition of deposit. Giving a carved figure to someone is a gift with weight. Placing an object in a cave niche is an act that connects the player to everyone who has ever placed something in the dark. The act of letting go is not loss — it's contribution. The player is adding to the world's accumulated meaning.

### 5.6 The Player as Maker

With sufficient Practice in carving and sufficient knowledge of the symbol systems, the player can make their own movable symbolic objects. This is a profound act. The player is not just reading the knowledge layer — they're contributing to it. They're making a mark that carries meaning, on a material they shaped with their own hands.

A figure the player carves and leaves at a sacred site becomes part of the world's persistence layer. If the game tracks it, another visit to that site years later might find the figure still there — weathered, perhaps, but present among the other deposits. The player has become part of the accumulation.

A figure the player carves and gives to someone becomes part of that person's possessions and potentially part of their identity. If the figure carries a symbol the recipient recognises, the gift has knowledge-layer implications — it signals that the player understands something, that they're part of the tradition.

A figure the player carves but doesn't mark — or marks with something of their own devising — is a different act entirely. It's the player beginning to create meaning rather than just receiving it. Whether the game should support player-originated symbols is a design question for future development. The implication is significant: a player who carves their own mark and deposits it at sacred sites across the landscape is leaving a trace that future archaeological investigation (by other characters, or in a theoretical future) would discover and puzzle over.

### 5.7 Deception and Uncertainty

Movable objects introduce uncertainty that fixed marks don't have. A spiral carved into a standing stone has been there for centuries — its age and provenance are established by its context. But a spiral carved on a pebble in the player's hand might have been made last week. The player can't always tell how old an object is or who made it.

This uncertainty is productive. A marked stone found at a sacred site might be an ancient deposit or a recent offering. A carved figure found on a path might have been lost yesterday or a generation ago. The player develops judgement about these things — reading the wear, the material, the context — but the judgement is never certain. This is faithful to how real archaeology works, and it adds a layer of interpretive skill to the player's engagement with the symbol system.

Objects from the arriving people's tradition introduce a further layer of uncertainty. A figure carved in a style the player doesn't recognise, bearing marks from a symbol system they haven't encountered — is this an object from across the water, or is it something from a native tradition they haven't yet met? The material might help (a metal tool suggests the arriving people) but isn't always decisive. The arriving people's symbolic objects, encountered without a translator's context, are puzzles within puzzles.

## 6. Word-Tellings

### 6.1 What Word-Tellings Are

Word-tellings are oral narratives — stories, histories, mythic accounts — carried in living memory by specific individuals. They are the primary vehicle for deep knowledge in The Barrow. Everything the deepest layers of the knowledge graph contain — the meaning of symbols, the significance of sacred places, the nature of the legendary figures, the history of the landscape, the understanding of the sacred — is encoded in word-tellings.

A word-telling is not a text. It has no fixed form. Every teller tells it differently — emphasising different episodes, using different names, including or omitting details based on their own understanding, their cultural context, and their assessment of the listener. The same core narrative, told by three different people, produces three different tellings that share a recognisable structure but differ in specifics.

This is not a flaw in the transmission. It is the nature of oral tradition, and it is a feature of the game. The player hears multiple versions and must hold them in mind simultaneously — not to determine which is "correct" (none is correct, or all are) but to triangulate toward a truth that no single version contains. The pattern emerges from the differences as much as from the similarities.

### 6.2 Core Narratives

The knowledge layer generates ten to fifteen core narratives — the deep stories of the world. Each core narrative:

**References specific landscape features.** The journey goes north along a specific route. The cave is a specific cave. The hill where the gods spoke is a specific hill. These references anchor the narrative to the physical world and give the player destinations to seek.

**Involves legendary figures.** Named in the Old Tongue, their names containing roots that encode their nature and their role. The woman who entered the cave. The man who carried fire from the mountain. The child who spoke to the dead. These figures are precedents for what the player might do — and their names, decoded, are clues.

**Contains symbols.** Each narrative references or is associated with specific visual marks. The spiral appears in the journey-north narrative. The concentric circles appear in the well narrative. These connections link the word-telling system to the symbol system.

**Has a truth at its centre.** Beneath the mythic language, each core narrative encodes something real — a piece of landscape history, a description of a real place, an account of a real astronomical phenomenon, a record of a real geological event. The tsunami that reshaped the coast. The volcanic eruption preserved in the name of a hill. The cave passage that genuinely connects two valleys. The truth is accessible to the player who combines the word-telling with direct observation and other knowledge.

**Connects to other narratives.** Core narratives share elements — the same legendary figure appears in two stories, the same sacred site is referenced by three narratives, the same symbol appears in multiple contexts. These connections create the web structure that makes the knowledge layer cohere.

### 6.3 Telling Variants

Each core narrative has multiple variants, generated by applying cultural and individual variation to the core structure. A variant differs from the core in:

**Names.** The same legendary figure might be called by different names in different cultural traditions, or the same name might be pronounced differently. The Old Tongue root is preserved across variants, but the surface form changes.

**Episodes.** Some variants include episodes that others omit. The chalk-south version of the journey-north narrative might include an episode about crossing the great river that the granite-west version lacks, because the western people don't know that river. An episode's presence or absence tells the player something about the teller's cultural position.

**Emphasis.** What matters in the story shifts between tellers. The elder in Breca's settlement emphasises the journey itself — the route, the landmarks, the dangers. The wise woman emphasises the transformation at the end — what the figure became, what they learned, what they sacrificed. The translator's version of the arriving people's telling emphasises the sacred destination — what they're travelling toward. Each emphasis reveals a different facet of the core truth.

**Detail.** Specific details change. The colour of a stone. The direction of a river. The number of days the journey took. Some of these changes are noise — the natural drift of oral transmission. Some are significant — the river's direction in one version contradicts another, and the contradiction points toward something the player can verify by visiting the place.

**Missing elements.** Some variants are fragmentary — the teller knows only part of the narrative, having learned it from someone who only knew part. A fragment is still valuable, because it might contain a detail that no other version has. The most important word-tellings might only be available in fragmentary form from most sources, with the fullest versions held by the deepest tradition-bearers — the wise woman, the elder who has devoted their life to remembering.

### 6.4 How Word-Tellings Are Delivered

The player receives word-tellings through the voice layer, which produces the actual text based on the core narrative, the variant parameters, the teller's trust level, the shared language depth, and the teller's disposition.

At low shared language depth, the player receives only the most basic elements — concrete nouns, simple actions, recognisable landscape references. "She speaks of a man who walked north. A long way. He came to a hill. Something happened at the hill." The understanding is skeletal but contains the core references.

At moderate depth, the player receives more — the narrative structure becomes clear, names are used, episodes are distinguishable, the emotional weight of the story is communicated. "She tells of Ardhu, who walked north from the sea cliffs, following the ridge track for many days. At the hill where the three rivers begin, he found the stone that speaks. What it said changed him."

At high depth, the player receives the full telling as the teller knows it — nuanced, allusive, rich with metaphor and cultural reference. The voice layer can draw on the player's common knowledge tags to include details that assume shared understanding. The player hears the word-telling as a member of the culture would hear it, with the cultural context present.

Through a translator, the player receives word-tellings from the arriving people — but filtered through the translator's understanding and competence. The translator might not have the vocabulary for certain concepts. They might paraphrase, simplify, or substitute familiar terms for foreign ones. The player receives an approximation of the original, and the approximation is itself informative — what the translator chose to translate and how they translated it tells you something about both cultures.

### 6.5 Word-Tellings and the Player's Memory

The player state records which word-tellings have been heard, from which teller, at which depth. When the player hears a second version of the same core narrative from a different teller, the game can note the resonance: "This story sounds like one Breca's elder told, but the details are different. The elder spoke of a hill with three rivers. The wise woman speaks of a hill with three wells."

The game does not resolve these differences for the player. It notes them and lets the player hold the ambiguity. The player's own memory and attention determine whether they pursue the discrepancy — visiting the hill to see whether it has rivers, wells, or both.

## 7. The Old Tongue

### 7.1 What the Old Tongue Is

The Old Tongue is not a language anyone speaks. It is the linguistic substrate beneath all the living languages of The Barrow — the remnant of a language (or languages) spoken by people who lived in this landscape before the current cultures developed. It persists in names: the names of rivers, hills, springs, gods, legendary figures, seasons, events. These names have been inherited by the living languages, worn smooth by generations of mouths, and are used daily by people who have no idea what they originally meant.

The Old Tongue is the deepest layer of the language system and one of the primary connectors within the deep knowledge graph. When the player learns to recognise Old Tongue roots in names, they gain access to a level of meaning that is invisible to the people around them — not because the people are ignorant, but because the names have become transparent through familiarity. Nobody questions what the river's name means. It's just what the river is called.

### 7.2 The Root System

The Old Tongue consists of fifty to one hundred roots, each with a core meaning. Roots combine according to simple rules to produce compound names. The root system is internally consistent — the same root always contributes the same core meaning, though the surface form might vary (vowel shifts, consonant softening, truncation in compound forms).

Examples of roots (illustrative, not final):

*dur* — stone, hard, enduring.
*lyn* — water, flowing, clear.
*mor* — death, boundary, passage.
*bren* — high, summit, exposed.
*cael* — hidden, covered, within.
*tarn* — still water, pool, reflection.
*gwen* — white, bright, holy.
*ard* — great, old, powerful.
*usk* — dark, deep, unknown.
*nant* — valley, sheltered, between.

A hill called *Brendur* combines *bren* (high) and *dur* (stone) — the high stone, the stony summit. A river called *Lyncael* combines *lyn* (water) and *cael* (hidden) — the hidden water, the underground stream. A god called *Ardmor* combines *ard* (great) and *mor* (death/passage) — the great passage, the lord of boundaries.

The player never sees a glossary. They encounter these names in the landscape and in word-tellings, and over time — if they're paying attention — they begin to notice that certain sounds recur in certain contexts. The hill and the standing stone on its summit share a root. The river and the spring that feeds it share a root. The legendary figure's name contains the same root as the cave they vanished into.

This noticing is one of the game's deepest pleasures. It can't be forced or shortcut. It happens through accumulation, through hearing dozens of names across many places and many tellings, and gradually perceiving the pattern beneath them.

### 7.3 How Old Tongue Names Are Generated

During world generation, the Old Tongue naming system is applied to every significant landscape feature, every god, every legendary figure, and every significant event referenced in the core narratives.

The process:

**Assign semantic tags.** Each feature or entity has semantic tags based on its nature. A hill: [high, stone, prominent]. A spring: [water, emerging, holy]. A legendary figure who entered a cave: [hidden, passage, transformation].

**Select roots.** Roots are selected from the root system based on semantic tags. A hill tagged [high, stone] draws roots *bren* and *dur*. A spring tagged [water, emerging, holy] draws roots *lyn*, *gwen*, and possibly a third.

**Compound names.** Roots are combined according to the compounding rules — typically two or three roots per name. The surface form is adjusted for euphony — vowel harmony, consonant clusters that feel pronounceable, length appropriate to the thing named (rivers tend to have shorter names, legendary figures tend to have longer ones).

**Apply linguistic drift.** The Old Tongue name is then filtered through the current regional language, producing a surface form that sounds like it belongs to the local speech while preserving the Old Tongue roots in recognisable (to the attentive player) form. The same Old Tongue name might surface as *Brendur* in one region and *Brindor* in another — the roots are the same but the local pronunciation differs.

**Ensure meaningful connections.** The generation verifies that names connected in the deep knowledge graph share roots appropriately. If a legendary figure is associated with a specific cave, their name and the cave's name should share at least one root. If a symbol system is associated with a specific concept (passage, water, fire), the names of the sites where that symbol appears should include roots related to that concept.

### 7.4 The Player's Relationship With the Old Tongue

The player never "learns" the Old Tongue as a skill. There is no vocabulary screen, no translation mechanic, no moment where the game says "you now understand the Old Tongue." Instead, the player accumulates exposure — hearing hundreds of names across the landscape and across word-tellings — and at some point, if they're attentive, recognition begins.

The voice layer supports this process subtly. When the player encounters a name that shares a root with a name they've heard before, the text might note the similarity: "The wise woman calls the hill Brendur. The sound reminds you of something — the stone the elder spoke of, the one called Durcael." The game doesn't explain the connection. It notes the resonance and trusts the player to wonder.

As the player accumulates more names and more resonances, the Old Tongue begins to function as a cipher — not a code to be cracked, but a layer of meaning that enriches everything. The name of a god resolves into a description of what the god does. The name of a legendary figure resolves into a compressed version of their story. The name of a place resolves into a description of what the place is — and sometimes that description reveals something about the place that the player didn't know. A hill whose name means "hidden water" might have an underground spring that the player hasn't discovered yet.

## 8. Sacred Sites

### 8.1 What Makes a Site Sacred

A site is sacred because people have treated it as sacred. The landscape layer provides the physical substrate — the spring, the cave, the hilltop, the stone circle. The habitation layer provides the people who use it. The knowledge layer provides the meaning.

But sacred sites in The Barrow have a property that most game sacred sites lack: **they were sacred before the current people arrived**. The stone circles were built by earlier generations. The barrows predate living memory. The marks in the deep caves predate the cultures that now live near them. Sacred sites are inherited — the current people maintain and use them, but they didn't create them, and their understanding of the original purpose may be partial, transformed, or wrong.

This means sacred sites have **layered meaning**. The current cultural common knowledge says the stone circle is for midsummer gathering. The word-tellings say the legendary figure Ardmor spoke to the gods here. The symbols on the stones predate both stories. The astronomical alignment predates the symbols. Each layer is a different depth of understanding, and the player can access each layer through different knowledge systems — common knowledge, word-tellings, symbol recognition, astronomical observation, Old Tongue name decoding.

### 8.2 Sacred Site Properties

Each sacred site in the knowledge layer has:

**Cultural significance.** What the local people believe about it, which is cultural common knowledge. This is the most accessible layer — any community member will share it once basic communication is established.

**Word-telling references.** Which core narratives reference this site, in which variants, with which details. This is deep knowledge, accessible through trusted relationships with tradition-bearers.

**Symbol presence.** Which marks are present at the site and which symbol systems they belong to. This is visually observable but only meaningful to a player with accumulated symbol knowledge.

**Astronomical alignment.** Whether the site has properties related to the solar or lunar calendar — alignment to solstice sunrise, to equinox sunset, to lunar extreme positions. This is discoverable through observation over time (being at the site at the right moment) and through word-telling references that hint at calendrical significance.

**Old Tongue name.** The name of the site in the Old Tongue, preserved in the local name and containing roots that encode the site's original significance. Accessible to the player who has developed Old Tongue recognition.

**Convergence potential.** The site's baseline capacity for supernatural convergence — how many systems can potentially align here. A simple standing stone at a path junction has low convergence potential. A stone circle aligned to midwinter sunrise, referenced in three core narratives, bearing symbols from two systems, named in the Old Tongue with roots that mean "speaking-passage-dead," and situated above a cave system with wight presence — that has very high convergence potential.

**Ritual associations.** What rituals are performed here, by whom, at what times. This is the most heavily gated knowledge — shared only at the deepest trust levels by individuals with ritual authority.

### 8.3 Discovery and Deepening

The player's experience of a sacred site deepens through the knowledge layers:

**First visit, low knowledge.** The site is physically present and physically impressive — a stone circle is visually striking regardless of what you know about it. Body Sense might register something. The voice layer describes the physical space.

**Return visits, growing knowledge.** The player recognises marks they've seen elsewhere. They recall a word-telling that mentioned this place. They notice an alignment they didn't see before because now they know to look for it. Each piece of common and deep knowledge they've accumulated adds a layer to the description.

**Deep engagement.** The player visits at the right time — midsummer dawn, midwinter dusk, full moon. They've heard the word-telling. They recognise the symbols. They've decoded the name. They may have been invited by someone with ritual knowledge to witness or participate in an observance. The convergence density rises, and the voice layer pushes toward the edges of material description.

This deepening is not a sequence of unlock events. It's a continuous process driven by the player's growing knowledge state. The same site is experienced differently every time, not because the site changes but because the player does.

## 9. Ritual

### 9.1 What Ritual Is

Ritual in The Barrow is maintenance — the rites keep the world functioning. The midwinter observance ensures the sun returns. The harvest rite ensures the land remains fertile. The rites of passage ensure the community survives the death of its members, the arrival of new members, and the transition of individuals from one life stage to another.

The people who perform these rituals believe they are necessary — not symbolic, not cultural, but necessary in the way that feeding the fire is necessary. The game respects this belief. It never reduces ritual to superstition or cultural decoration. Within the logic of this world, the rites matter.

### 9.2 Types of Ritual

**Calendar rituals.** Performed at specific times — solstice, equinox, lunar phases, seasonal transitions. These are the most public rituals, often involving the whole community and sometimes drawing people from multiple settlements. Midsummer at the stone circle. Midwinter at the barrow. First fruits at the harvest. These are cultural common knowledge — everyone knows they happen and roughly what they involve.

**Rites of passage.** Marking transitions — birth, the passage from child to adult, partnership, death. These are performed for specific individuals and involve the community. They are partly public knowledge and partly gated — the general structure is common knowledge, but the specific practices and words may be known only to those with ritual authority.

**Healing rituals.** Performed by specialist practitioners — the wise woman, the healer. These involve specific knowledge of herbs, of the body, of the sacred, and of the words and gestures that accompany the practical treatment. The line between medicine and ritual is not drawn in this world.

**Appeasement and maintenance.** Rituals performed to maintain the relationship between the community and the forces of the world — the land, the water, the dead, the gods. Offerings at springs. Deposits at sacred sites. The careful words spoken before entering a cave, before crossing a river, before felling a tree. These are pervasive — they run through daily life like a thread — and they're partly cultural common knowledge and partly specialist knowledge.

**The darkness ritual.** The cave passage in darkness, documented in the landscape model and the game description. This is the most intense ritual in the game, and it requires the deepest preparation — knowledge of the cave, mastery of the darkness, understanding of what the passage means, and the willingness to enter the dark without light.

**Passage.** The game's ultimate ritual. Entering the barrow, undergoing transformation, emerging changed. This is the threshold that the entire knowledge layer points toward, and it requires the convergence of everything the player has learned.

### 9.3 Ritual Knowledge in the Graph

Ritual knowledge is distributed across the deep knowledge graph as fragments, each connected to specific sites, specific times, and specific practitioners.

A ritual fragment might contain:
- What must be done (an action, a gesture, a positioning of the body).
- What must be said (specific words, possibly in the Old Tongue or in a formulaic register of the local language).
- What must be brought (offerings, materials, objects).
- When it must be done (calendar time, astronomical alignment, lunar phase).
- Where it must be done (at a specific site, facing a specific direction, in a specific position relative to the stones or the passage).

No single source provides all fragments of a given ritual. The player assembles ritual knowledge from multiple sources — a word-telling that describes what was done, a practitioner who knows the words, a symbol on a stone that indicates the correct position, an astronomical observation that reveals the correct timing. The assembly of ritual knowledge is itself a form of the game's core activity — gathering fragments, holding them in mind, recognising how they connect.

### 9.4 Participation and Observation

The player's access to ritual is gated by the relationship layer:

**Observation from distance.** No trust required. The player might see a community gathering at a stone circle from a hilltop, without understanding what's happening. This is a sightline event — visible but opaque.

**Observation from proximity.** Low trust. The player is tolerated at the edges of a public calendar ritual. They see what happens but aren't part of it. The voice layer describes what they observe.

**Witness.** Moderate to high trust. The player is invited to be present as a recognised observer. They see more — the specific gestures, the words, the sequence. They might be told what things mean as they happen.

**Participation.** Deep trust plus ritual knowledge. The player is invited to participate — to perform an action, to speak a word, to bring an offering. Participation is rare and significant. It changes the player's identity (they are now someone who has participated in this rite) and it may unlock further knowledge (the experience of performing a ritual reveals things that observation cannot).

## 10. The Impossible Objects

### 10.1 Principles

No more than five in the entire world. Each is unique. Most players will never see any of them — they may only hear of them in word-tellings. The impossible objects are evidence of The Old Knowledge manifesting as physical things — objects that do not belong in this world, that defy the material rules of the landscape, and that raise questions the game refuses to answer.

Scarcity creates meaning. If there are five in the world, each is an event. Possessing one changes the player's identity in ways that are not entirely positive — people who know you have it will want it, fear it, or fear you.

### 10.2 The Five Objects

**The Singing Blade.** A short knife or dagger made of meteoric iron — though nobody in this world knows what iron is or where it came from. The blade is a dark, slightly bluish metal, heavier than bronze, harder than anything anyone has ever encountered. It holds an edge that never seems to dull. When drawn across stone, it produces a faint ringing tone — hence the word-telling references to "the blade that sang." The metal is cold to the touch even on a warm day.

The blade is not large or dramatic. It's small enough to hold in one hand, practical in shape, clearly made by someone with metalworking knowledge, but made in a style that matches no known tradition — not the arriving people's bronze-work, not anything from the native cultures. It looks *made*, but not by anyone from this world. Its surface has a subtle patterning — Widmanstätten lines, the crystalline structure of meteoric iron, though the player and the people of this world have no name for this.

The Singing Blade is kept in the deepest chamber of a sacred cave in the limestone country, visited only by the tradition-bearer at midwinter. Word-tellings across multiple cultures reference it — "Ardmor carried something that sang when drawn, and no edge could stand against it" — but its location is one of the most closely guarded secrets in the game. The tradition-bearer who guards it considers it a burden, not a treasure. It frightens them. They keep it because someone must.

**The Warm Stone.** A stone, roughly the size of two fists held together, that is warm. Not faintly warm — body-warm, as if it had been held by someone or sat by a fire. But it hasn't. It's always warm. In winter, it's noticeably warmer than its surroundings. In the coldest conditions, frost melts in a circle around it. It's a dull greyish-green colour, smooth, slightly heavier than it looks.

Nobody can explain why it's warm. It's not volcanic (the nearest volcanic activity is referenced only in the oldest Old Tongue names). It doesn't glow. It doesn't change. It's simply, impossibly, warm. The people who keep it treat it with a mixture of reverence and unease. It has been used in healing — held against the body of someone with a fever, placed against a wound — and the cultural common knowledge in its region says it has power. Whether it actually heals or whether the belief does the healing is a question the game does not answer.

The Warm Stone is kept by a hermit-healer in the western granite mountains. The hermit chose isolation partly because of the stone — people came to them constantly, wanting its warmth, wanting its healing, and the hermit found the attention unbearable. Reaching the hermit requires climbing into harsh, remote terrain. Earning their trust requires understanding why they left.

**The Amber Eye.** A piece of amber, translucent golden-orange, roughly the size of a hen's egg, polished smooth by handling over countless generations. Inside the amber, perfectly preserved, is a creature. It is not an insect. It is not any animal that anyone in this world recognises. It has too many limbs, arranged in a symmetry that doesn't match anything alive. It has what might be eyes, or what might be something else entirely. It is very small — the whole creature fits within the amber — but the detail is extraordinary. Every limb, every filament, every surface is preserved.

The Amber Eye is profoundly unsettling to look at closely. The creature inside is not from this world, or not from this age of the world — it's ancient beyond comprehension, from a time so remote that life itself looked different. The people who know of it call it various things in different cultures — the trapped god, the sleeping eye, the thing-that-watches. Some word-tellings suggest it sees the person looking at it. Some suggest it dreams, and its dreams leak into the world.

The Amber Eye is kept at a sacred site near the coast — a stone circle where the eldest tradition-bearer of a fishing community guards it. It is shown only at the dark of the moon, and only to those the tradition-bearer judges worthy. The amber itself is warm to the touch — though whether this is a property of the amber or a property of what's inside is another question the game leaves open.

**The Unbroken Pot.** A fragment of pottery — roughly a quarter of what was once a large vessel — that is harder, thinner, and more finely made than any pot in this world. The clay is a colour nobody can replicate — a deep, even red that doesn't match any local clay source. The firing temperature was far beyond what any kiln in this world can achieve. The surface bears a glossy finish that native potters cannot explain.

And on the outer surface, in a band around what would have been the vessel's shoulder, there are symbols. Not marks from any known symbol system. Not the spirals, not the cup marks, not the concentric circles. Something else — geometric, precise, repeated in a pattern that suggests writing but isn't quite writing. Nobody can read them. Nobody recognises them. They are evidence of a knowledge system that is entirely foreign to everything in this world.

The Unbroken Pot was found in a sealed chamber of a barrow on the chalk escarpment — a chamber that hadn't been opened in living memory, possibly in centuries. The barrow is one of the oldest constructions on the chalk, and the pot was placed there by the builders. It is now in the keeping of the elder of the largest settlement on the chalk, who considers it proof that the barrow-builders possessed knowledge that has been lost. The pot is the most intellectually disturbing of the impossible objects, because its symbols suggest that someone, somewhere, at some time, had a form of knowledge — possibly writing — that has vanished from the world entirely.

**The Night Tooth.** A tooth — or something shaped like a tooth — made of a material that is not bone, not stone, not metal, not wood. It is jet-black, with a surface that seems to absorb light rather than reflect it. It's roughly the length of a finger and slightly curved, tapering to a sharp point. It is cool to the touch but not cold. It is extraordinarily light — almost weightless.

Nobody knows what animal it came from. It doesn't match any known creature's dentition — too large for most predators, wrong shape for a herbivore, wrong curve for a fish. The material doesn't scratch, doesn't chip, doesn't wear. It is harder than flint but behaves differently when struck — no spark, no ring, just silence and the sense that the impact was absorbed.

The Night Tooth is the most mysterious of the five objects because it provokes the fewest associations. The blade is a tool. The stone has healing properties. The amber contains something visible. The pot bears symbols. The Night Tooth is simply *there* — an object that exists, that shouldn't exist, and that defies categorisation. Word-tellings reference it as something carried by legendary figures at moments of crossing — held in the mouth during passage through caves, clutched in the hand during the darkness ritual. It is associated with thresholds and transitions — not as a tool but as a companion, something you take with you when you go somewhere you might not come back from.

The Night Tooth is kept in the water-lands, in the oldest crannog settlement, submerged in a pool of still water inside a sealed chamber beneath the platform. The water-people believe it fell from the sky in their grandmothers' grandmothers' time. Reaching it requires not only the deepest trust with the water-people but understanding of why it is kept under water — which connects to the water-lands' word-tellings about the drowned land, about things that sink and things that rise, about the boundary between what is above and what is below.

### 10.3 Properties Shared by All Five

Each impossible object has:

**Material impossibility.** Something about the object that cannot be explained by the game world's material knowledge. The impossibility is specific and demonstrable, not vague or subjective. Someone handling the object knows immediately that it doesn't belong.

**Word-telling references.** Each object is referenced in at least one core narrative, usually obliquely. The word-tellings tell the player that the objects exist and hint at their nature and location, without being treasure maps.

**Location and guardian.** Each object is at a specific site, in the care of a specific individual or community. The guardian treats the object as a sacred responsibility. The access requirements are among the deepest trust thresholds in the game.

**Connection to The Old Knowledge.** Each object is a physical node in the deep knowledge graph, connecting to word-tellings, to symbols, to sacred sites, and ultimately to the threshold of Passage. The objects are evidence that the boundary between this world and whatever lies beyond it is not absolute — things have crossed over, and they persist as anomalies in the material world.

**Atmosphere of unease.** Each object is respected but also feared by its guardian. The Singing Blade frightens the tradition-bearer. The Warm Stone drove its keeper to isolation. The Amber Eye is shown only in darkness. The Unbroken Pot is intellectually disturbing. The Night Tooth is kept under water. Nobody is comfortable with these things. They are honoured and they are dreaded.

### 10.4 The Player's Relationship With Impossible Objects

The player may hear of an impossible object in a word-telling and spend months pursuing it — travelling to the region, building the relationships, earning the trust, finally seeing it. The moment of encounter is one of the game's peak experiences — the voice layer describes the object with precision and lets the impossibility land without commentary.

Possessing an impossible object (if the guardian entrusts it to the player, which would require the deepest trust in the game) changes everything. It's a visible identity marker that other people react to — some with awe, some with fear, some with desire. It's a burden as much as a treasure. And it connects the player to The Old Knowledge in a way that nothing else does, because they are carrying physical evidence of something that should not exist.

## 11. Pattern and The Old Knowledge

### 11.1 Pattern

Pattern is the node in the system graph where multiple deep knowledge streams converge. It is the moment when the spiral on Breca's panel, the mark on the cairn, and the bronze amulet resolve into a single connected meaning. It is the moment when two word-tellings about the same legendary figure, told by different people in different cultures, triangulate toward a truth that neither teller possesses.

Pattern is not a revelation. It's not a cutscene or a text box that says "you have discovered the meaning of the spiral." It's a state of the player — a critical mass of accumulated deep knowledge where connections become visible, where the Old Tongue names resolve into meaning, where the landscape reveals its hidden coherence.

The game supports Pattern by noting resonances in the voice layer — "this mark reminds you of..." and "the name sounds like..." — but it does not assemble the pattern for the player. The player's mind does the work of connection. The game provides the fragments. The Pattern node in the system graph is reached not through a game mechanic but through the player's own cognitive act of recognition.

### 11.2 The Old Knowledge

The Old Knowledge is what the land remembers. What the stones were placed to mark. What the word-tellings are truly about. It is the layer beneath everything — beneath the living cultures, beneath the sacred practices, beneath the word-tellings and the symbols, beneath even the Old Tongue. It is the understanding that the barrow-builders had, that the mark-makers had, that the cave-wight observers had, that has been fragmented and scattered and degraded but never fully lost.

The Old Knowledge is not a piece of information. It's not a sentence the player reads or a secret they're told. It's a state of understanding — a coherent framework that makes sense of the fragments the player has gathered. It's the moment when the player understands why the barrow was built where it was, why the stones are aligned as they are, why the word-telling describes what it describes, why the name means what it means, and how all of these things are connected to the passage between states that the game calls Passage.

The knowledge layer does not generate The Old Knowledge as content. It generates the fragments, the connections, and the convergence conditions. The Old Knowledge emerges — or doesn't — in the player's mind.

### 11.3 Threshold Conditions for Passage

The knowledge layer defines the conditions under which Passage becomes possible. These are not a checklist but a convergence of understanding:

**Pattern recognition.** The player has connected enough deep knowledge threads to perceive the underlying coherence. This is tracked by the system as a density of connections in the player's knowledge state — how many pattern connections they've made, how many threads they've followed to depth.

**Ritual knowledge.** The player has assembled enough ritual fragments to understand what must be done at the threshold — what actions, what words, what timing, what orientation.

**Sacred site identification.** The player has identified the barrow that is the threshold — the specific site where Passage is possible. This identification comes through convergence of word-tellings, symbols, Old Tongue name decoding, and possibly the guidance of the wise woman or another deep tradition-bearer.

**Sacrifice readiness.** The player has something to lose. Something they've built and valued throughout the game — a relationship, an aspect of identity, a way of understanding the world. Passage requires giving something up, and the game must know that the player has something meaningful to give. This is the hardest condition to define mechanically, and it may need to be handled through the voice layer and the player's choices at the threshold rather than through a system check.

**Convergence.** At the moment of Passage, all the factors of the convergence system must align — the right place, the right time, the accumulated knowledge, the ritual preparation, and the physical and psychological state of the player. Passage is the apex of convergence — the moment when more systems align simultaneously than at any other point in the game.

## 12. Knowledge Layer Generation

### 12.1 When Knowledge Is Generated

The knowledge layer is generated after the landscape and habitation layers, because it must reference specific sites, specific people, and specific landscape features.

**At world creation:** Core narratives are defined and anchored to the landscape. Symbol systems are created and distributed. The Old Tongue root system is applied to landscape features, gods, and legendary figures. Thread structures are established — which nodes connect to which, where the entry points are, how threads intersect.

**At district generation:** When a new district is generated (as the player approaches), the knowledge layer populates it with specific knowledge content — symbol instances at sites, word-telling fragments distributed to generated individuals, common knowledge appropriate to the region and culture. The content is drawn from the pre-existing thread structures, ensuring consistency with the wider world.

**Never:** The knowledge layer does not generate content at the moment scale. What the player reads is produced by the voice layer, drawing on the knowledge layer's data. The knowledge layer provides the facts. The voice layer provides the expression.

### 12.2 Ensuring Coherence

The most important requirement for knowledge generation is coherence — the threads must hold together across the entire game world. A symbol that appears at three sites must be the same symbol, referencing the same underlying meaning, connected in the graph. A word-telling told by two different people must be recognisable as variants of the same core narrative. An Old Tongue name must use the same roots with the same meanings everywhere it appears.

This coherence is achieved by generating the knowledge layer's structure globally (at world creation) and filling in the details locally (at district generation). The structure — which core narratives exist, which symbol systems exist, which Old Tongue roots mean what, how threads connect — is fixed at world creation. The details — which specific site has which specific symbol instance, which specific individual carries which specific word-telling fragment — are determined at district generation, constrained by the global structure.

### 12.3 The Knowledge-Responsive Bias

When generating knowledge content for new districts, the system can subtly bias the placement of content based on the player's current knowledge state. If the player has been following the spiral thread, newly generated districts in their path are slightly more likely to contain spiral-related content — not guaranteed, not inevitable, but nudged.

The bias is light and plausible. It doesn't change what exists in the landscape. It influences which of several possible placements the generation selects. A district that could contain either a spiral instance or a concentric-circle instance is slightly more likely to contain the spiral if the player has been following that thread. The district contains the same number of knowledge nodes either way — only the specific selection is biased.

This bias must be subtle enough that the player never detects it as a system. The world should feel like it's producing serendipitous connections, not serving content. Most discoveries should feel like genuine exploration. The bias just tilts the odds slightly in favour of meaningful discovery over meaningless noise.

## 13. Summary

The knowledge layer is the web of meaning that transforms The Barrow from a landscape simulation into a world worth knowing. It operates through two strata:

**Common knowledge** — universal, regional, cultural, and specialist — is the foundation. It's absorbed through proximity and time, it differs between cultures, and it provides the grammar that makes deep knowledge legible.

**Deep knowledge** — symbols, word-tellings, Old Tongue names, sacred site significance, ritual fragments, impossible objects, and pattern connections — is the web the player discovers through effort, trust, and attention. It's structured as a graph of threads that the player follows across the landscape, gathering fragments that connect to other fragments in a web that leads deeper.

The knowledge layer's core mechanisms are:

**Symbols** that are seen before understood, that recur across distances, and that resolve into meaning as the player's knowledge grows.

**Word-tellings** that vary between tellers, that encode landscape truth in mythic language, and that triangulate toward understanding through comparison.

**The Old Tongue** root system that connects names across the landscape, encoding meaning that is recoverable through accumulated exposure and attention.

**Sacred sites** with layered significance accessible through different knowledge systems at different depths.

**Ritual** as maintenance — gated by trust, assembled from fragments, and culminating in the darkness passage and Passage itself.

**Impossible objects** that defy the material rules of the world, that are referenced in word-tellings, and that connect to The Old Knowledge.

**Pattern** as the convergence point where multiple knowledge streams resolve into coherent understanding, and **The Old Knowledge** as the deepest layer — what the land remembers, emerging not as delivered content but as a state of the player's own understanding.

The knowledge layer is generated globally at world creation and detailed locally at district generation. It maintains coherence through global structure and achieves specificity through local filling. It subtly biases new content toward the player's current knowledge interests, creating the sense of meaningful discovery without the feeling of being served content.

Everything in the knowledge layer is designed to reward the same thing: patient, attentive, curious engagement with a world that has more depth than any single visit reveals.
