# The Barrow — The Habitation Layer

## How the world is peopled: settlements, individuals, cultures, trade, the wights, the arriving people, and the biological realities of being human.

---

## 1. Overview

The habitation layer takes the generated landscape and populates it with living beings. It sits above the landscape layer and below the knowledge layer. It is generated after the landscape and constrained by it — settlements exist where the landscape supports them, trade routes follow viable paths, cultures are shaped by geology and geography.

The habitation layer must produce a world that feels inhabited rather than populated. The difference is specificity. A populated world has NPCs distributed at intervals. An inhabited world has particular people in particular places for particular reasons, with relationships to each other that predate the player and continue without the player's involvement. The player enters a world that is already in motion.

---

## 2. Population and Distribution

### 2.1 How Many People

The Barrow's world is sparsely inhabited by modern standards but not empty. Real late-Neolithic Britain supported a population estimated at somewhere between 100,000 and 300,000 people across an area equivalent to the game world. That's roughly one to three people per square mile on average — but averages are misleading because distribution is extremely uneven.

The game doesn't need to simulate all of these people. It needs to represent enough of them that the world feels inhabited, and it needs to distribute them correctly. Most people the player will never meet. They exist as the implied population behind the settlements — the figures moving in the distance, the smoke from unseen hearths, the worn paths and managed landscapes that tell the player someone lives here.

The game explicitly represents:

**Named individuals.** People the player can meet, talk to, build relationships with. Perhaps three to eight per settlement, plus mobile individuals. The total number of named individuals in the game world might be several hundred, but the player will only ever know a fraction of them.

**Settlement populations.** Each settlement has an implied population larger than its named individuals. A settlement with five named characters might represent a community of twenty to forty people. The unnamed people are present as background — figures seen at a distance, sounds of activity, the general life of the settlement — but are not individually modelled.

**Implied habitation.** Signs of human presence without specific people — managed woodland, paths, cleared land, old fire circles, middens, fences, fish traps. These tell the player that people live in this landscape even when no one is visible.

### 2.2 Distribution

Population follows carrying capacity, which follows geology.

**Chalk south.** Highest density. The downland supports grazing and the valleys support settlement. Well-watered by springs. Flint abundance supports a craft economy. Settlements are relatively close together — a player might pass through two or three inhabited districts in a day's walk. The chalk south has the most complex social structures, the most trade, and the most cultural dynamism, partly because of density and partly because the arriving people from across the water are landing here.

**Clay lowlands.** High density but less visible. The rich soil supports the most people per area, but the dense forest hides settlements. A player in the clay lowlands might not know a settlement exists until they stumble into the clearing where it sits. Settlements are connected by river rather than by ridgeway, and the river is the primary social axis.

**Limestone country.** Moderate density. The fertile dales support settlement; the exposed pavement and higher ground do not. Population clusters in the valleys with long stretches of uninhabited upland between them. Settlements are more isolated than in the chalk south, which gives them a more insular character.

**Sandstone country.** Moderate to low density. The heathland is less productive than chalk downland or clay forest. Settlements are smaller and more widely spaced. People here are more self-reliant and less connected to the wider trade networks.

**Slate and granite — the western mountains and coast.** Low density. Harsh terrain, thin soil, limited farmland. Settlements are small — perhaps a single extended family group — and widely separated. The coastal settlements may be somewhat larger because of fishing. These are the most isolated communities in the game, and they may have preserved the oldest traditions precisely because of that isolation.

**The water-lands.** Low density, but highly distinctive. The population is spread across crannogs, raised mounds, and stilt settlements scattered through the vast wetland. Each settlement is small but connected to others by boat. The water-people are a distinct cultural group with their own knowledge and traditions, and accessing their settlements requires either a boat or knowledge of the tidal paths.

**The glacial north.** Almost uninhabited. A few hardy individuals or tiny family groups living in temporary camps at the ice margin. The player might walk for days without seeing any sign of human habitation. Any person encountered here is significant — a prospector, a fugitive, a visionary following a word-telling that says something lies beneath the ice.

### 2.3 The Emergence Region

The area where the player begins is generated with slightly higher density and more variety than its regional context would normally produce. This isn't dramatically higher — it shouldn't feel artificial — but it ensures that the player encounters people, paths, and sites within the first few sessions regardless of where they emerge. The emergence region should contain at least one small settlement, at least one mobile individual who passes through periodically, and enough landscape features to sustain early exploration.

## 3. Settlements

### 3.1 What a Settlement Is

A settlement is a site in the landscape model that contains a community of people. It ranges from a single homestead (one extended family, one or two structures) to a walled town (dozens of families, multiple structures, defensive works, specialist craft areas). Between these extremes lie most settlements: a cluster of three to ten roundhouses near a ford, a spring, or good farmland, with associated structures — animal enclosures, storage pits, a communting area, perhaps a meeting place or a designated sacred space.

Every settlement is placed according to the historical landscape layer — at a location that was suitable for habitation one to two hundred years ago. The tensions between historical placement and current conditions (see Landscape Model, Section 10) give settlements a lived-in quality that newly optimised placement would lack.

### 3.2 Settlement Properties

Each settlement has:

**Location and reason.** Why this settlement is here — the ford, the spring, the farmland, the defensive hilltop, the fishing ground, the flint beds. The reason should be legible to an observant player, and the tensions between the historical reason and the current landscape should also be readable.

**Size.** Expressed as an approximate population and a number of visible structures. Size determines the settlement's social complexity, its economic capacity, and how many named individuals it supports.

Small (homestead): 5-15 people, 1-2 structures, 2-3 named individuals. A single extended family. Self-sufficient but with little surplus. Limited specialist knowledge.

Medium (hamlet): 15-40 people, 3-6 structures, 4-6 named individuals. Two or three family groups. Some specialisation — a dedicated flint-knapper, a healer, someone who manages trade. Connected to the wider world through trade paths.

Large (village): 40-100 people, 7-15 structures, 5-8 named individuals. Multiple family groups with defined social roles. Significant specialisation — craft areas, storage, a meeting space. An elder or leader figure. Active trade connections. Possibly defensive features.

Walled town: 100+ people, many structures within a boundary. 6-10 named individuals. The most complex social structure in the game. Multiple specialists. Active trade. Political relationships with surrounding settlements. The apothecary in the walled town represents this category.

Water-lands settlement (crannog/raised mound): 10-30 people, adapted structures on water. 3-5 named individuals. Boat-dependent. Specialist water knowledge. Connected to other water-lands settlements by channel rather than by path.

**Material character.** What the settlement is built from, which is determined by geology. Timber and thatch on clay lowlands. Dry stone and thatch in limestone and granite country. Wattle and daub in mixed-geology areas. The water-lands settlements use timber, reed, and raised platforms. Material character is part of the settlement's visual identity and tells the player about the geological context.

**Economic profile.** What the settlement produces, what it needs, what it trades. A chalk-south settlement produces flint tools and surplus grain and needs timber (which doesn't grow well on chalk). A clay-lowland settlement produces timber and pottery and needs flint. A granite settlement produces very little surplus. A coastal settlement produces fish, salt, and shell, and needs everything else. The economic profile drives trade relationships and determines what the player can exchange here.

**Cultural identity.** Which cultural group the settlement belongs to, including language variety, pottery style, building style, god-names, calendar observances, and common beliefs. Settlements near cultural boundaries may show mixed influences.

**Social structure.** How authority works in this settlement. In small settlements, the oldest or most experienced person has informal authority. In larger settlements, leadership may be more defined — an elder, a chieftain, a council. The social structure determines who the player needs to engage with to be accepted, and who has the authority to share or withhold knowledge.

**Sacred associations.** Whether the settlement has a sacred site nearby — a standing stone, a spring, a barrow, a grove — and what the settlement's relationship is with that site. Some settlements are oriented around a sacred feature. Others exist for purely practical reasons with sacred sites elsewhere in the district.

**State of change.** Whether the settlement is stable, growing, declining, or recently disrupted. A settlement near a shifting ford might be in slow decline. A settlement near newly cleared farmland might be growing. A settlement recently affected by a fire, a death, or the arrival of the people from across the water might be in a state of disruption. The state of change affects the mood of the settlement, the disposition of its inhabitants, and what the player encounters on arrival.

### 3.3 Settlement Life

Settlements have daily and seasonal rhythms that the player observes and participates in.

**Daily rhythm.** Morning activity — tending animals, starting fires, preparing food. Daytime work — farming, gathering, crafting, building, hunting. Evening gathering — food preparation, eating, talking, maintenance. Night — rest, firelight, the settlement draws inward. The player arriving at different times of day encounters different scenes and different availability of people.

**Seasonal rhythm.** Planting and growth in spring. Abundance and gathering in summer. Harvest and preparation in autumn. Endurance and firelight in winter. The seasonal rhythm affects what the settlement is doing, what it needs, what it has to trade, and the mood of its people. A settlement in late autumn is busy with preparation — drying food, repairing shelters, gathering firewood. A settlement in midwinter is hunkered down, and a stranger arriving is both more notable and more welcome (or more unwelcome, depending on trust and resources).

**Calendar events.** Midsummer, midwinter, equinoxes, lunar observances. The settlement gathers for these. Visitors arrive — people from other settlements, mobile individuals. Calendar events are the moments when settlements are most socially active and when the player has the best opportunity to meet people, hear word-tellings, witness ritual, and build relationships. They're also when the men from across the water might appear at nearby sacred sites.

### 3.4 Settlement Generation

Settlements are generated at the district scale, placed according to landscape suitability and the historical landscape layer. The generation algorithm uses rules:

Place settlements near water sources (springs, rivers, lakes).
Place settlements near fords on significant rivers.
Place settlements near productive land (the definition of which varies by geology).
Place settlements with access to building materials.
Cluster settlements loosely in productive areas; leave harsh terrain empty.
Ensure settlements are connected by paths to at least one other settlement, unless they're deliberately isolated (hermitage, outcast community, ice-margin camp).

The size of a settlement is determined by the carrying capacity of its surroundings and its position in the trade network. Settlements at trade route junctions tend to be larger. Settlements in marginal areas tend to be smaller.

## 4. Individuals

### 4.1 What the Game Needs From an Individual

Every named individual in the game is a potential carrier of knowledge, a potential relationship, and a potential thread in the player's developing quest. But they must also feel like people — not like knowledge dispensers with personality wrappers. The player should encounter them as human beings first and as game functions not at all.

This means individuals need enough specificity to be memorable and enough consistency to be credible across multiple encounters, but they don't need psychological depth in the novelistic sense. The player's relationship with an individual is built primarily through presence, action, and the gradual deepening of communication — not through dialogue trees or backstory revelations.

### 4.2 Individual Properties

Each named individual has:

**Name.** Drawn from the linguistic traditions of their region, using the phonological rules established by the language generation system. Names should sound like they belong to the same language as the place-names around them.

**Age and physical character.** Not precise years but a life stage — young adult, mature, elder — and a few physical traits that the voice layer can reference consistently. The tall woman. The man with the damaged hand. The elder whose hair is white. These are recognition features, not detailed character descriptions.

**Role in the community.** What they do. The pig-tender, the flint-knapper, the healer, the elder, the person who manages trade, the person who tends the sacred site, the boat-builder, the hunter. Role determines daily routine, specialist knowledge, what they have to trade, and where the player encounters them.

**Disposition.** A small set of temperamental qualities that shape interactions. Not a personality model — three or four parameters that the voice layer uses to colour descriptions and dialogue.

Openness: how readily they engage with strangers. Ranges from wary to welcoming.
Generosity: how readily they share — food, knowledge, time. Ranges from guarded to generous.
Curiosity: how interested they are in the player and the player's origins. Ranges from incurious to fascinated.
Temperament: their general emotional tone. Calm, anxious, irritable, cheerful, melancholy.

These parameters are fixed at generation and don't change. A wary person becomes trusting of the player through the relationship layer, but their baseline wariness with strangers remains. A generous person shares readily once trust is established; a guarded person shares more reluctantly even at the same trust level.

**Knowledge.** What they know, drawn from the knowledge layer. This includes:

Common knowledge appropriate to their culture and region (automatically assigned based on cultural group and location).
Specialist knowledge appropriate to their role (the healer knows herbs; the flint-knapper knows flint beds; the elder knows the settlement's history).
Word-telling fragments they carry — not everyone carries word-tellings, and those who do carry only fragments unless they're specialist tradition-bearers.
Symbol knowledge — whether they recognise specific marks and what they know about them.
Local landscape knowledge — paths, features, dangers, useful sites within their home district and possibly adjacent districts.
Knowledge of other people — who lives where, who trades with whom, who is related to whom. This is the social map, and it's one of the most practically useful things an individual can share.

**Routine.** Where they are at different times of day and season. Routines are simple and repeating — the pig-tender is at the enclosure in the morning, at the settlement in the evening. The healer gathers herbs in the valley during summer and stays close to the settlement in winter. The elder is almost always at the settlement. Routines determine when and where the player can encounter each individual and give the settlement the feeling of ongoing life.

**Relationships with other individuals.** Within the settlement and beyond. These are simple and defined at generation: alliance, kinship, rivalry, tension, deference, dependency. They don't change dynamically (the game doesn't simulate NPC social drama), but they create a social texture that the player can observe and navigate. Gaining the trust of the elder might make the healer more wary, if the two are in tension. Bringing a gift that pleases one person might be observed by another who draws conclusions.

**Needs and desires.** What the individual currently needs or wants. Simple, practical things — a particular material for a project, food during scarcity, help with a task, news of a relative in another settlement. These create opportunities for the player to offer reciprocity and build trust. They change over time, driven by season and circumstance. A flint-knapper always needs good flint but particularly needs it after their supply was damaged. A healer needs specific herbs that grow in a place she can't safely reach alone.

### 4.3 Individual Generation

Individuals are generated as part of settlement generation. The process:

Determine the settlement's size and how many named individuals it supports.
Assign roles based on settlement size and type — every settlement has someone who manages food, someone who tends structures, and someone with authority. Larger settlements add specialists.
Generate names from the regional language system.
Assign dispositions randomly within plausible ranges for the settlement's cultural context.
Assign knowledge from the knowledge layer — common knowledge automatically, specialist knowledge based on role, word-telling fragments distributed sparsely (not everyone carries them; tradition-bearers are rare and significant).
Generate routines based on role and season.
Generate inter-individual relationships within the settlement.
Generate needs based on role, season, and settlement state.

The generation should ensure that every settlement has at least one individual who is notably open to strangers (providing the player an entry point), at least one who is notably guarded (providing social texture), and if the settlement is large enough, at least one who carries a word-telling fragment or has knowledge that connects to the deep knowledge web.

### 4.4 How Individuals Change Over Time

Individuals are mostly stable — their properties don't change dramatically during the game. But some things shift:

**Needs change** with seasons and circumstances. What someone needs in summer is different from winter.

**Mood and state change.** A person might be grieving after a death in the settlement. A person might be anxious about the approaching winter. These temporary states affect how the voice layer describes them and how receptive they are to the player.

**Aging.** Over the course of a long game (multiple years), individuals age. An elder might die. A young adult might assume a new role. A child might grow into a named individual. These changes are slow and tied to the game's calendar, and they create the sense that the world moves forward.

**Death.** Individuals can die — of illness, of age, of accident, of violence. Death is not common but it's present. When a named individual dies, the settlement changes. Their role might be filled by someone else or might go unfilled. Their knowledge, if not passed on, is lost. The player who returns to find that the wise woman has died while they were away has lost something that cannot be recovered — and that loss is one of the game's most emotionally significant events.

Death connects to The Near Dead in the system graph. The player encounters grief, tended graves, the customs of death. A person's absence is as meaningful as their presence.

## 5. Mobile Individuals

Not everyone is tied to a settlement. Mobile individuals move through the landscape on their own logic, and encountering them is different from visiting a settlement — less predictable, more contingent, often more significant.

### 5.1 The Wise Woman

A specific archetype that may appear in various forms across the world — the person who carries deep knowledge and lives somewhat apart from normal settlement life. Not every region has one. Where one exists, they are singular: there is one wise woman in a given territory, not a category of identical NPCs.

The wise woman has:

A territory — a valley, a stretch of coast, a limestone region. She moves within it but rarely leaves.
Seasonal routines tied to the land — gathering at specific places in specific seasons, visiting sacred sites at calendar moments.
Deep knowledge — word-tellings, ritual knowledge, symbol interpretation, understanding of the sacred landscape. She is one of the primary repositories of deep knowledge in the game.
High trust threshold — she doesn't share easily. The player must earn her confidence through time, presence, reciprocity, and demonstrated understanding. She's not hostile — she may be warm and welcoming — but she discerns between a casual visitor and someone ready to hear what she knows.
Knowledge of the cave-wights — she may be one of the few people who has practical experience of them, and what she knows might include knowledge originally derived from observing their behaviour.
A role in the community — even though she lives apart, she's connected to the nearest settlements. People come to her for healing, for advice, for ritual. She's respected but also somewhat feared. Her relationship with the settlement is complex and the player can observe it.

The wise woman is one of the game's most important individuals. Finding her, building trust with her, and earning access to her knowledge is one of the primary pathways into the deep knowledge layer. But she's not the only pathway, and a player who never finds a wise woman can still reach the deep layer through other routes.

### 5.2 Traders

Traders move between settlements along trade routes, carrying goods, news, and reputation. They are the game world's communication network — the mechanism by which information and reputation travel at the speed of human walking.

A trader has:

A route — a set of settlements they visit in a roughly regular cycle. The cycle might take weeks or months, depending on the distance.
Trade goods — materials from the settlements they've visited. Flint from the chalk. Timber from the clay lowlands. Fish from the coast. Pottery from settlements known for it. Copper ornaments from places with access to native copper. Occasionally, something rarer.
News — what's happening in the settlements they've visited. Who's thriving, who's struggling, who's died, what's been built, what's been destroyed. News is imperfect and delayed — the trader reports what they saw when they were there, not what's happening now.
Reputation knowledge — stories about people, including the player. A trader who has visited a settlement where the player is known carries that knowledge onward. This is one of the primary mechanisms for reputation propagation.
Social position — traders are generally welcomed because they bring useful things, but they're also outsiders in every settlement they visit. Their relationships are broad but shallow. They know many people but are intimate with few.
Language competence — traders tend to be better communicators than most people, because their livelihood depends on it. A trader might have wider shared language than a typical settlement-dweller, spanning more of the regional linguistic gradient.

Traders create natural opportunities for the player. Meeting a trader on a path is a chance to trade goods, hear news, learn about distant settlements, and understand the economic geography of the region. A trader might also be the first person to tell the player about the wise woman, about a sacred site, about the men from across the water, or about something strange that happened in a distant place.

### 5.3 The Men From Across the Water

A small group — perhaps five to eight individuals in the entire game world, arriving in one or two groups. They are not traders in the conventional sense, though they bring trade goods (including bronze). They are the representatives of an arriving culture — the Beaker-equivalent people who are beginning to land on the south coast with new materials, new knowledge, and new gods.

The men from across the water are:

**Culturally alien.** Their language is from a different family. Their beliefs are different. Their material culture is different. They represent a world the player has never seen and probably never will.

**Following their own word-tellings.** They've come here for reasons rooted in their own tradition — perhaps seeking a legendary site, a sacred artefact, a promised land. Their route through the landscape is dictated by their own knowledge, which may be imperfect or mythologised. They travel to the stone circle at midsummer because their word-tellings say they must. They push north because the route their forebears took, according to their tradition, goes north.

**Carrying bronze and the knowledge to make it.** They understand the alloying of copper and tin, which is essentially arcane to the native people. A bronze blade or amulet is not just a trade good — it's evidence of a knowledge system that the native world doesn't possess. The men carry a small number of bronze objects and possibly the raw materials and tools to work bronze.

**Inaccessible without translators.** The player cannot communicate meaningfully with the men from across the water without a translator. The language barrier is absolute. Even with a translator, communication is imperfect and mediated — what the translator chooses to translate, how they interpret concepts, what they leave out.

**Significant wherever they go.** Their presence at a settlement or a sacred site is an event. People notice them. Their bronze is admired, feared, coveted. Their rituals at the stone circle are observed and discussed. Word of their movements travels through the trade networks. The player is likely to hear about the men from across the water before they encounter them.

**Temporary.** They don't settle (yet — this is the very beginning of a process that will take generations). They move through the landscape on their own schedule and eventually depart, either returning across the water or continuing their journey beyond the player's reach. They might return the following year, or they might not. Their impermanence makes every encounter with them precious.

The men from across the water serve several functions in the game. They're a source of deep knowledge (their word-tellings, accessible only through translators, encode information about the wider world and about traditions that predate the native cultures). They're a catalyst for cultural tension (their presence disrupts established relationships and beliefs). They're evidence that the world extends beyond the game's boundaries. And their bronze is a thread that can lead, if followed far enough, to the discovery of tin in the west and the theoretical possibility of native bronze-making.

### 5.4 The Translators

Three or four individuals in the entire game world who can mediate between the native languages and the arriving tongue. Each translator is unique, with their own history, position, and motivations.

Possible translator backgrounds:

A child of a mixed union — born to a native mother and a man from across the water who stayed longer than the others. They grew up between two worlds and belong fully to neither.

A native individual of unusual linguistic ability who attached themselves to the arriving people out of curiosity and spent enough time with them to acquire functional (but imperfect) competence in their language.

A person from across the water who arrived young, was fostered by a native family, and has grown up speaking both languages but with stronger ties to the native world.

A person of high status in a south-coast settlement whose role as intermediary has given them political power but also made them suspect — they know things others can't verify, and their loyalty is permanently in question.

Each translator has:

**Dual cultural knowledge.** They carry cultural common knowledge from both traditions, making them repositories of information that almost nobody else has.
**Imperfect competence.** No translator is perfectly fluent in both languages. Each has gaps, biases, and preferences that affect what they translate and how.
**A social position.** Valued by both sides but trusted by neither. Translators are politically significant and socially isolated.
**Their own agenda.** Each translator wants something — acceptance, power, knowledge, connection, resolution of their own divided identity. Their agenda affects their willingness to help the player and what they choose to share.
**A location and routine.** Translators are found near the places where the native world and the arriving world intersect — the south coast settlements, the trade routes, the sacred sites where the men from across the water gather.

Finding a translator is one of the game's significant milestones. The player goes from hearing about the men from across the water to being able to communicate with them (imperfectly, through mediation), which opens an entire branch of the knowledge layer that was previously inaccessible.

### 5.5 Outcasts and Hermits

Individuals living outside settlements — in caves, in remote shelters, on the margins of the inhabitable world. Each has a reason for their isolation.

**Voluntary hermits.** People who chose solitude — for spiritual reasons, for grief, for the pursuit of knowledge that requires separation from community. They might be wise figures in their own right, carrying knowledge that's different from the wise woman's because it's been developed in isolation.

**Exiles.** People expelled from their communities for transgression. They survive on their own skills and carry the knowledge they had before exile, plus whatever they've learned in isolation. An exile might be desperate, dangerous, or simply lonely. Their knowledge of their former community might be useful to the player, but their reputation means that association with them carries social risk.

**Fugitives.** People fleeing something — a feud, a failed ritual, a broken obligation. They might be found in the most remote places — the ice margin, the deepest forest, the most inaccessible cave. Encountering a fugitive is encountering someone at the extreme edge of the human world.

Outcasts and hermits are generated sparsely — perhaps one per several districts. They serve as discovery content for the player who explores remote areas, and they often carry unusual knowledge precisely because they've lived outside the normal social structures. A hermit in a cave might know the cave system better than anyone in the nearest settlement. An exile might carry a word-telling fragment from a distant region that nobody local has heard.

## 6. Trade and Exchange

### 6.1 The Gift Economy

The Barrow's world has no money. Exchange operates through gift economy, barter, and reciprocal obligation. This is not a simplified game version of trade — it's how pre-monetary societies actually work, and it has specific properties that affect gameplay.

**Gifts create obligation.** Giving someone something useful creates an expectation of future reciprocity. The wise woman remembers that you brought herbs. Breca remembers that you helped mend the panel. These aren't transactions — they're investments in social bonds. The obligation isn't explicit and it doesn't expire.

**Barter is situational.** Direct exchange of goods happens, but it's negotiated based on immediate need and perceived value, not on fixed prices. A fine arrowhead might be worth a pot of rendered fat in chalk country where flint is abundant, but worth a season's hospitality in granite country where flint is unobtainable. The player who understands these asymmetries has discovered one of the game's deepest practical systems.

**Value is relative and geographic.** Every material has a different value in different places, determined by local abundance or scarcity. This creates the fundamental incentive for trade — moving things from where they're common to where they're rare. Flint flows north and west from the chalk. Timber flows south and east from the clay lowlands. Fish and salt flow inland from the coast. Copper ornaments flow from wherever native copper is found.

**Some things can't be traded.** The impossible objects are outside the economy. Sacred knowledge is not bought or sold. A relationship cannot be purchased with gifts — gifts contribute to trust, but trust requires time and presence and cannot be shortcut.

### 6.2 Trade Networks

Settlements are connected by trade relationships that predate the player. These are not formal agreements — they're established patterns of exchange maintained by traders and by direct contact between settlements.

A trade network has:

**Routes.** The paths traders walk, connecting settlements in rough circuits. Routes follow the landscape's paths — ridgeways, valley floors, coastal routes. The major trade routes are the most travelled paths in the game and the places where the player is most likely to encounter other people while walking.

**Goods.** What flows along each route. Flint from the chalk south along the ridgeway north. Pottery from the clay lowlands along the rivers. Salt from the coast inland. Jet from the northern coast south along the mountain spine track. Each route has characteristic goods that the player can observe and participate in.

**Information.** Trade routes carry news as well as goods. A trader who walks a route every few months carries information about what's happening at each stop. This information is delayed and imperfect but it's the primary way that distant settlements know about each other — and about the player.

**Reputation.** The player's reputation travels along trade routes. An action at one settlement reaches the next settlement on the route after the next trader passes through. This means reputation propagation has a specific geography — it follows the trade routes, not a uniform radius around the player. The player might be well-known along the ridgeway they've travelled but completely unknown in the valley just over the ridge.

### 6.3 What the Player Can Trade

The player participates in the trade system through direct exchange. They can offer:

**Gathered materials.** Herbs, food, raw materials found through foraging. The value depends on what's locally scarce.

**Crafted objects.** Flint tools, pottery, bone implements. Quality depends on the player's Practice level, and high-quality crafted objects are among the most valuable trade goods in the game.

**Labour.** Helping with tasks — mending, building, gathering, carrying. Labour builds reciprocal obligation and trust simultaneously.

**Knowledge.** Telling someone about a path, a resource, a danger, or a distant settlement. In this world, information has trade value because it's scarce and useful.

**Rare materials.** Crystals, pigments, jet, copper — things that are geographically specific and always valuable far from their source.

The player cannot trade for the things that matter most — trust, deep knowledge, ritual access, the wise woman's confidence. These are earned, not purchased. But trade creates the conditions for earning them — bringing something useful is a form of reciprocity that opens the door to relationship.

## 7. Cultural Groups

### 7.1 What a Cultural Group Is

A cultural group is a collection of settlements and individuals that share a language variety, a set of common beliefs, a material style, and a body of word-tellings. Cultural groups are shaped by geography — separated by mountains, connected by rivers, unified by shared sacred sites.

A cultural group is not a nation. It has no borders, no government, no army. It's a gradient of shared practice that fades at the edges and overlaps with neighbouring groups. Two settlements in the same cultural group share recognisable pottery styles, common god-names, and mutual word-tellings. A settlement at the boundary between two groups might show elements of both.

### 7.2 Cultural Group Properties

Each cultural group has:

**A language variety.** A branch of one of the three or four root language families, with regional accent and vocabulary. People within the group understand each other readily. People at the group's edges have partial mutual intelligibility with neighbouring groups. People across major linguistic boundaries cannot understand each other without sustained contact.

**God-names and sacred practices.** Which gods are honoured, at which sites, at which times, in which ways. The gods are diverse — some masculine, some feminine, some neither, some tied to specific places, some more abstract. Cultural groups that share geography might share some god-names but differ on others. The differences in sacred practice between groups are one of the sources of cultural tension in the game.

**Material style.** Pottery shapes and decoration, building techniques, tool-making traditions, clothing, ornamentation. Material style is the most visible marker of cultural identity and one of the things the player learns to read. A pot from the chalk south looks different from a pot from the granite west, and the player who carries one into the other's territory is carrying a signal.

**Word-telling traditions.** The stories this group tells. Some word-tellings are shared across groups (with variant details). Some are unique to one group. The distribution of word-tellings across cultural groups is one of the knowledge layer's most important structures, because the player's ability to hear multiple versions of the same story — and triangulate truth from the differences — depends on encountering the same core narrative in different cultural contexts.

**Calendar observances.** Which calendar events are observed and how. Midsummer is significant to most groups but observed differently. Midwinter practices vary. The lunar observances of the water-people are different from the mountain people's solar focus. Calendar observances are cultural common knowledge that the player absorbs by being present for them.

**Attitudes toward the arriving people.** How this group regards the men from across the water and their bronze. Ranging from fascination to hostility, with most groups somewhere in between — wary curiosity, pragmatic interest in trade, anxiety about what the newcomers represent. The south-coast groups have the most direct contact and the most complex attitudes. The remote western and northern groups may barely be aware the arrivals exist.

### 7.3 Cultural Group Generation

Cultural groups are generated at the regional scale, emerging from the interaction of geography and the language family placement. The generation algorithm:

Places language families across the landscape, following major geographical divisions.
Subdivides language families into regional varieties, following secondary geographical features (river basins, coastal stretches, mountain valleys).
Assigns cultural properties — god-names, material styles, word-telling traditions — to each regional variety, ensuring both distinctiveness (each group feels different) and overlap (some traditions are shared, creating the gradient quality of real cultural boundaries).
Places cultural boundaries at geographical transitions — mountain passes, wide rivers, dense forests — where sustained contact is difficult.
Creates boundary zones where cultural groups overlap and mix.

The generation must ensure that no cultural group feels like a monolith. Each group should contain internal variation — the settlement at the edge of the territory is different from the settlement at the centre, because it's been influenced by its neighbours.

## 8. The Wights

The cave-wights and the small folk are placed by the habitation layer but handled differently from humans. They are presences rather than characters, and the game's systems treat them accordingly.

### 8.1 The Cave-Wights

**Territory.** Each cave-wight presence occupies a territory centred on a limestone cave system. The territory extends to the surface landscape immediately around the cave entrances — the hillside above, the valley below, the woodland nearby. A territory might span one to three districts.

**Population.** Not individually modelled. A cave-wight territory has a presence — a simple flag indicating that cave-wights are active here. The game does not track individual cave-wights, their movements, or their relationships. They are a force, not a cast of characters.

**Traces.** The cave-wight presence manifests as observable traces in the landscape and in caves within their territory:

Trails that end impossibly at rock faces.
Signs of habitation in deep cave chambers that humans rarely reach — bone arrangements, marks that predate human habitation, evidence of fire in places where humans would struggle to bring fuel.
Sounds in caves — not alarming, but present. A quality of being observed.
Animal behaviour — animals in cave-wight territory behave slightly differently, as if responding to a presence the player can't see.
Body Sense alertness — the player feels something in cave-wight territory, without knowing what.

**Encounter probability.** Extremely low. Most players will never have a direct encounter with a cave-wight. Those who do — through prolonged, patient, non-threatening presence in the territory, combined with deep cave exploration — have one of the rarest experiences in the game. A direct encounter is not a conversation. It's a sighting — a figure in the deep cave that is clearly not human, observed for a moment before it withdraws into darkness. The implications are profound but the event itself is brief.

**Deep encounter.** Almost impossibly rare. A player who has spent a very long time in cave-wight territory, who has demonstrated absolute non-threat, who has navigated the caves extensively, who has perhaps performed the ritual darkness passage in their territory — this player might achieve something approaching mutual awareness with the cave-wights. Not trust in the human sense. Not communication in the human sense. But a state where the cave-wights tolerate the player's presence in their deep caves, and where the player might find things left for them — a passage revealed, a mark illuminated, a route through the underground that connects to a distant surface location. This is endgame content available to almost no players.

### 8.2 The Small Folk

**Territory.** Each small-folk presence occupies a territory in the warm, wet places — reed beds, warm springs, dense wet woodland, the margins of the water-lands. Territories are smaller than cave-wight territories and more fragile.

**Population.** Like the cave-wights, not individually modelled. A presence flag for the territory.

**Traces.** Different from cave-wight traces:

Glimpses at the edge of vision — a small figure that's gone when you look directly. The voice layer describes these as ambiguous — it might have been an animal, a shadow, a trick of light.
Sounds — not quite language, not quite birdsong. Rhythmic, almost musical. Present in the warm wet places at certain times.
Paths through marsh that follow no logic visible to humans — but that might follow underground water courses or geological features beneath the surface.
Disturbed vegetation that doesn't match animal behaviour — things moved, things gathered, things arranged in ways that are purposeful but alien.
Body Sense registers something in small-folk territory — not the watchfulness of cave-wight territory, but a quality of aliveness. The warm wet place feels more inhabited than it looks.

**Encounter probability.** Rare but somewhat higher than cave-wights, because the small folk are less adept at hiding and less deliberate about avoiding humans. A player who spends significant time in the warm wet places, who moves quietly, who tarries — they might glimpse one. A clear sighting is still very rare.

**Relationship possibility.** Even less defined than with cave-wights. The small folk may become accustomed to the player's presence in their territory, leading to more frequent glimpses, closer approaches, perhaps objects left near the player's resting place — a smooth stone, a peculiar shell, a reed woven into a shape. These are not communications in any language the player can interpret. They might be gifts. They might be warnings. They might be play. The ambiguity is permanent.

### 8.3 Wight Placement

Wight territories are placed during district generation, constrained by geological requirements (cave-wights require limestone cave systems; small folk require warm wet environments). Not every suitable habitat has wight presence — the wights are rare, and most districts have no wight territory at all.

The generation should ensure that wight territories are distributed sparsely across the game world. Perhaps ten to fifteen cave-wight territories and five to ten small-folk territories in the entire world. Their placement should feel organic — connected to the landscape's specific properties rather than evenly distributed.

Wight territories should not be flagged or announced. The player discovers them through accumulated observation — Body Sense, traces, word-tellings from local people. A player who has heard the wise woman speak of the cave-wights and then enters a territory where Body Sense registers something unfamiliar is connecting the knowledge layer to the habitation layer through their own experience.

## 9. The Arriving People

The men from across the water deserve treatment as a cultural phenomenon, not just a set of NPCs. They represent the beginning of a process that will eventually transform the entire world of The Barrow — but from the player's perspective, they are a handful of strangers who carry impossibilities.

### 9.1 What They Represent

The arriving people are the first contact between two worlds. The native cultures of The Barrow's landscape have been developing in relative isolation for millennia, shaped by local geology, local ecology, local tradition. The arriving people come from a place where different conditions have produced different knowledge — metalworking, different agricultural practices, different gods, different understanding of the sea and what lies beyond it.

From the native perspective, the arriving people are deeply ambiguous. They bring beautiful and useful things (bronze). They bring knowledge that nobody else has. They participate in sacred practices at the same sites, suggesting shared deep traditions. But they also speak an incomprehensible language, worship unfamiliar gods, and behave in ways that don't follow local social rules. They are simultaneously fascinating and threatening.

### 9.2 Their Presence in the Game

The arriving people are present as one or two small groups, totalling perhaps five to eight individuals. They arrive by sea on the south coast, and they move through the landscape following routes dictated by their own word-tellings. Their movements are seasonal and somewhat predictable — they arrive in late spring, travel through summer and autumn, and depart before winter.

They carry:

**Bronze objects.** Blades, amulets, pins, small tools. Not many — perhaps a dozen objects across the entire group. Each one is extraordinary to the native people.
**Raw materials.** Copper, possibly tin, the tools and knowledge for metalworking.
**Their own word-tellings.** Oral traditions from their homeland that reference the landscape of The Barrow — stone circles, particular coastlines, specific routes. These tellings suggest that there has been contact before, possibly generations ago, and that the arriving people have a tradition of coming here.
**Their own gods.** Different from the native gods, but with possible deep connections that suggest common origins in the very distant past. This is discoverable through the translators and through pattern recognition.
**Their own common knowledge.** A complete cultural framework that is opaque to the player except through translation.

### 9.3 The Player's Experience of Them

The player is likely to hear about the men from across the water before encountering them. A trader mentions strangers on the south coast. Breca tells of men with bronze at their throats who visited the elder. A word-telling fragment mentions people who come from across the sea.

The first encounter is potentially one of the game's most memorable moments — meeting people who are clearly human but utterly foreign. The language barrier is absolute. Communication is reduced to gesture, expression, the showing of objects. The bronze catches the light. The player can observe but not converse.

The translators are the key to going deeper. Through a translator, the player can learn fragments of the arriving people's word-tellings, understand their route, hear their god-names, and begin to detect the deep connections between their traditions and the native traditions. This is some of the richest material in the knowledge layer — and it's filtered through the imperfect, biased, agenda-laden mediation of the translator, which means the player is never sure how much they're receiving accurately.

### 9.4 Tension and Change

The arriving people create tension wherever they go. Settlements that welcome them gain access to bronze and new knowledge but risk cultural disruption. Settlements that reject them miss the opportunity but preserve their traditions. The player observes these tensions and may be drawn into them — asked to mediate, to translate their own understanding, to take sides.

Over the course of the game, the arriving people's influence spreads, slowly. A south-coast settlement adopts a new pottery style influenced by the arriving culture. A sacred site begins to be used in a new way. A word-telling gains a new episode that references the bronze-carriers. The cultural landscape of the south is changing, and the player can witness this change in progress. Further north and west, the old ways persist, and the tension between innovation and preservation is one of the emotional currents of the game.

## 10. Settlement Death

Settlements can die. Disease, a devastating winter, a fire that destroys the food stores before the cold, or — rarely — violence. This is modelled as a rare event generated by the game's weather, season, and circumstance systems. Perhaps a few settlement extinctions occur across the whole game world over the course of years. It should never feel routine.

### 10.1 What Happens

When a settlement dies, it becomes an abandoned site. The structures remain but decay. Untended animals go feral or die. Paths begin to grow over. Over seasons and years, the clearing shrinks as the landscape reclaims it. But traces persist — fire circles, postholes, middens, the shapes of where roundhouses stood.

An abandoned settlement is one of the most atmospherically potent sites in the game. It connects to The Near Dead and The Far Dead in the system graph — the recent dead, whose graves are fresh, whose absence is felt. A player who returns years later finds a ghost of a place, and that ghost becomes part of the landscape's archaeological record, joining the older abandoned settlements that the historical landscape layer already produced.

### 10.2 Consequences for the Player

If the player knew these people — if they'd built trust with Breca, and Breca's settlement is the one that's gone — the loss is devastating and permanent. The trust they built, the shared language, the things they were told — these persist in the player state as knowledge, but the source is extinguished. The player cannot go back and ask the wise woman to clarify that word-telling, because the wise woman is dead.

This reinforces the game's theme that knowledge is fragile and mortal, carried in the mouths and minds of living people. And it reinforces the value of the time the player spent there — those sessions with Breca weren't just progress toward a goal. They were time with a person who is now gone.

### 10.3 Partial Death

Not every catastrophe kills everyone. Disease might take half a settlement's population, leaving survivors diminished, grieving, and struggling. A harsh winter might kill the eldest and the youngest, removing tradition-bearers and the next generation simultaneously. Partial death is more common than total extinction and in some ways more painful to witness — the settlement persists, but changed, reduced, haunted by absence.

The game should handle this through modifications to the settlement's state — named individuals removed, mood shifted to grief, needs increased, activity reduced. The player arriving at a settlement recently struck by disease encounters a different place from the one they knew.

## 11. Disability and Difference

### 11.1 Presence in the World

People with disabilities exist in The Barrow because people with disabilities have always existed. The game generates individuals with physical and cognitive differences as a natural part of population generation — not as special cases but as part of the normal range of human variation.

A person born without lower limbs. A person who lost a hand in an accident. A person who is blind. A person whose mind works differently — pattern-focused, socially atypical, prone to states of deep absorption. A person who cannot speak. A person whose body is twisted by a condition from birth. These individuals are present in settlements, with roles, with knowledge, with relationships, with stories.

The voice layer describes them without sentimentality and without special emphasis. They are people. Their difference is part of who they are, not the entirety of who they are.

### 11.2 The Warp: Disability as Hidden Ability

In The Barrow's world, disability is not seen as disadvantage. It is understood as a mark of hidden ability — a sign that the person has a different relationship with the world, one that carries compensatory depth.

The cultural logic varies by community but the principle is widespread:

**Physical difference as divine exchange.** A person born without lower limbs is understood to have made a sacrifice before birth — they gave something to the gods, or the gods took something from them, and in return they received a gift that whole-bodied people don't possess. What the gift is depends on the individual and the cultural context, but the assumption is that it exists. This person might be a tradition-bearer, a seer, a maker of extraordinary objects. Their physical constraint has given them a different relationship with stillness, with the ground, with what it means to stay when others go. In a game built around the tarrying mechanic, this is a profound and recognised gift.

**Cognitive difference as different seeing.** A person whose mind works in ways the modern world might label as autistic is recognised in this world as someone who sees what others miss. They might be the person who reads the symbols, who hears the patterns in the Old Tongue names, who sits at the sacred site for hours while others come and go and notices the thing that only reveals itself to sustained, unwavering attention. The word the local culture uses for this person is not a diagnosis — it's a role. Adept. Seer. The one who watches. It's respected, sometimes feared, always valued.

**Acquired difference as survival story.** A person who lost a hand, an eye, the use of their legs — they carry the event visibly, and the scar is part of their story. They survived something. They adapted. They might have developed extraordinary compensatory skill, or they might have shifted from a physical role to a knowledge role. The community sees both the loss and the adaptation, and both are respected.

**Difference and the sacred.** Across many real pre-modern cultures, the shaman or ritual specialist is frequently someone who is different — who stands apart from the normal social structure because they occupy a role that requires not being normal. In The Barrow, this connection between difference and sacred knowledge should be present without being formulaic. Not every disabled person is a shaman. Not every shaman is disabled. But the culture recognises a relationship between unusual perception and access to the sacred, and individuals with cognitive or sensory differences are more likely to be found in roles connected to the deep knowledge layer.

### 11.3 Mechanical Representation

Disability is a property of specific generated individuals. It affects:

**Routine.** A person with limited mobility doesn't travel far. A blind person navigates their home settlement flawlessly but doesn't range widely. These constraints shape where and when the player encounters them.

**Specialist knowledge.** A person whose difference gives them a particular kind of perception may carry knowledge that others in the settlement don't — symbol interpretations, word-telling fragments, ritual knowledge, pattern recognition. Their difference is part of why they know what they know.

**Role.** Their role in the settlement reflects both their capabilities and the culture's understanding of their difference. The person who watches. The tradition-keeper. The maker of fine, precise things. The one who speaks to the dead.

**How others relate to them.** Other individuals in the settlement have attitudes toward disabled members that range from deep respect to fearful deference to matter-of-fact acceptance. These attitudes are part of the settlement's social texture.

Disability does not reduce an individual's value as a knowledge source or a relationship. In many cases, it increases it. The player who overlooks the person sitting quietly by the fire because they can't walk is missing one of the settlement's most important minds.

## 12. Sex and Intimacy

### 12.1 Sex as Part of Life

In a world without modern privacy norms or the moral frameworks imposed by later religions, sex is a natural and visible part of life. People in these settlements live closely. Couples share sleeping spaces. Sex is understood by adults and children alike as a normal part of being alive.

The game doesn't describe sex graphically. The voice layer handles intimacy with the same spare, concrete, non-explicit prose it uses for everything — suggesting rather than depicting, trusting the reader. The player sees a community where sex is woven into daily life as background texture: pairings, attractions, the dynamics of desire and partnership observed as part of settlement life.

### 12.2 Sex and Social Structure

Partnerships in this world are partly about desire and partly about social alliance. A pairing between individuals from two settlements creates a kinship bond with trade and alliance implications. Children born of such pairings connect communities. The man from across the water who fathered a child with a local woman created one of the translators — a single sexual encounter that has shaped the geopolitics of the south coast. Sex creates consequences that ripple through the habitation layer.

Partnership structures vary by cultural group. Some cultures practise something resembling monogamy. Others are more fluid. Some have formal partnership rituals; others don't. The player observes these variations as part of cultural common knowledge — how this community handles partnership and reproduction is part of what makes them who they are.

### 12.3 Sex and the Sacred

Many pre-modern cultures integrated sexuality into sacred practice — fertility rites, seasonal observances connected to growth and renewal, sacred unions at specific calendar moments. In The Barrow, this is part of cultural common knowledge. The player learns that certain rituals have sexual dimensions, that fertility is sacred, that the earth's capacity to produce life is connected in the culture's understanding to human sexuality.

The game acknowledges that ritual sex exists as part of the sacred landscape without depicting it explicitly. The player might witness the beginning of a rite and understand its nature, or hear about it through word-tellings, or be told that what happens at the stone circle on midsummer night is between the participants and the gods.

### 12.4 Sex and the Player

The player's relationship with a specific individual might, at sufficient trust and shared language depth, include physical intimacy. This is not a goal to be pursued or a reward to be earned — it's a natural possibility within a deep relationship, one that the game handles with restraint.

The voice layer describes intimacy with economy: "She takes your hand. The fire burns low. Later, you watch the stars from the doorway of the roundhouse while she sleeps." That is sufficient. The player understands.

Intimacy has consequences. It deepens the relationship. It affects identity — other people in the settlement notice and draw conclusions. It might create a child, which changes the player's relationship with the community fundamentally. And if the relationship later deteriorates or the player leaves for a long time, the loss is felt more keenly because of the depth that was reached.

The player's sexual encounters, if any, are always consensual. This is a design boundary. Consent is communicated through the relationship layer — intimacy only becomes a possibility at trust levels that imply genuine mutual willingness.

### 12.5 Queerness

Pre-Roman Britain almost certainly included same-sex relationships and gender identities that don't map onto modern categories. The Barrow's world includes this as part of its social texture.

Some individuals are partnered with people of the same sex. Some individuals don't fit neatly into their culture's gender expectations. The cultural response varies — some communities have specific honoured roles for people whose gender expression is non-conforming, connected to the principle that difference carries hidden ability. Others are less accommodating. The variation is part of the world's specificity.

The player's own sexuality is not defined at character creation. It emerges, like everything else about the player, through choices. If the player builds a deep relationship with an individual of the same sex, and the relationship reaches the point where intimacy is possible, that's a possibility the game supports without commentary. The game treats queerness the way it treats everything else — as part of the specificity of being human, observed without judgement.

### 12.6 Design Boundary: Sexual Violence

The game does not include sexual violence or coercion as content. The world contains danger, death, loss, harsh conditions, and cultural tension. It does not include rape, sexual assault, or coerced sex as witnessed or experienced events. This is a design boundary. The game can acknowledge that this world contains power imbalances and the potential for coercion without making sexual violence part of the player's experience.

This boundary is noted as part of a broader discussion of power dynamics that is yet to be developed (see Section 14).

## 13. Reproduction and Population Change

### 13.1 Children

Children exist in this world. Settlements have them. Babies are born, children grow, some survive and some don't. Infant and child mortality in a pre-modern society is high — perhaps a third of children don't survive to adulthood. This is a fact of life that the people of The Barrow live with, and it shapes their relationship with death, with the sacred, and with the future.

Children are present in settlements as background — the player sees them, hears them, observes them as part of the settlement's texture. They are not named individuals in the early game, but a child the player sees as an infant on their first visit might, years later, become a young adult with their own role, knowledge, and name. This is one of the most powerful expressions of the passage of time in the game.

The birth of a child is a community event. The death of a child is a community grief. Both are things the player might witness, and both connect to the sacred — birth rites and death rites are part of the cultural common knowledge the player absorbs through presence.

### 13.2 Population Dynamics

Over the course of a long game, the population of each settlement changes through the balance of births, deaths, arrivals, and departures.

**Growth.** Good conditions — mild winters, adequate food, no disease — allow settlements to grow. A hamlet might add a roundhouse. A new family might arrive from a struggling settlement nearby. A growing settlement might eventually split, with a group breaking off to establish a new homestead. The player might witness this process — the scouting, the building, the first season in the new place.

**Decline.** Bad conditions — harsh winters, disease, failed harvests, the departure of key individuals — cause settlements to shrink. A village becomes a hamlet. A hamlet becomes a homestead. A homestead is abandoned. The decline might be gradual enough that the player only notices it over multiple visits — fewer people, quieter, a roundhouse that hasn't been repaired.

**Succession.** When individuals age and die, their roles must be filled. An elder's knowledge must be passed to someone, or it's lost. The game models this as a transfer probability — if the elder spent time with a younger individual (observable by the player as a mentoring relationship), the knowledge partially transfers when the elder dies. If the elder died suddenly, or if there was no suitable inheritor, the knowledge is gone. This is one of the most consequential things that can happen in the game world, because it means the knowledge layer can lose content permanently.

**New named individuals.** Children grow into adults and become named individuals with generated properties. This happens on the game's calendar — a child born in the first year of play might become a young adult five or six years later (assuming accelerated but not instant growth appropriate to the game's pacing). These new individuals inherit cultural common knowledge from their community and may develop specialist knowledge based on their assigned role.

### 13.3 Regional Trajectories

Different regions of the game world have different population trajectories.

The **chalk south** may be growing, partly because of fertile conditions and partly because the arriving people's influence is stimulating change and trade.

The **clay lowlands** are relatively stable — the rich soil sustains consistent populations.

The **western mountains** are stable or slowly declining as conditions remain harsh and young people occasionally leave for more hospitable areas.

The **water-lands** are declining as conditions worsen — water levels rise, habitable ground shrinks, and some families are relocating to the mainland.

The **glacial north** is very slowly gaining its first permanent settlements as the ice retreats and hardy pioneers push into new territory.

These trends are not dramatic within a single game. They're perceptible over years — a shift in the number of settlements, the size of communities, the balance of activity between regions. They're part of the world's deep texture, the sense that the landscape and the people on it are both in motion.

## 14. Power (To Be Developed)

How power is held, exercised, and resisted in The Barrow's world is a subject that requires focused treatment. It intersects with the habitation layer at every level — who leads a settlement and why, how decisions are made, how disputes are resolved, how the arriving people's bronze gives them leverage, how the wise woman's knowledge is a form of power, how the translators' position gives them unique influence, how taboos are enforced, how exile works, and how violence (rare but real) shapes social relationships.

The design boundary around sexual violence (Section 12.6) is part of this broader discussion. The game needs a coherent treatment of coercion, authority, obligation, and resistance that doesn't shy away from the realities of power in a pre-state society while maintaining design boundaries around the kinds of violence the player experiences.

This section will be developed in a future design session.

## 15. Summary

The habitation layer produces:

**Settlements** ranging from single homesteads to walled towns, placed according to the historical landscape layer, with economies shaped by geology, social structures shaped by size, and cultural identities shaped by geography. Each settlement has daily and seasonal rhythms, needs and surpluses, and connections to other settlements through trade. Settlements can grow, decline, partially die, or be entirely lost — and each of these trajectories is visible to the player over time.

**Named individuals** with roles, dispositions, knowledge, routines, relationships, and needs. Generated to be specific enough to be memorable, consistent enough to be credible, and differentiated enough to create social texture. Subject to slow change over time, including aging and death. Individuals include people with physical and cognitive disabilities, who are understood in this world as carrying hidden ability and who often hold knowledge and roles connected to the deep knowledge layer.

**Mobile individuals** including wise women, traders, the men from across the water, translators, and outcasts. Each type serves a distinct function in the game — knowledge repositories, information networks, cultural disruption, linguistic bridges, and marginal perspectives.

**Trade networks** operating through gift economy and barter, with geographic value asymmetries that reward the player who learns what's common where and what's rare where. Trade routes carry goods, news, and reputation.

**Cultural groups** defined by language variety, sacred practices, material style, and word-telling traditions. Cultural boundaries are gradients, not lines. The contradictions between cultural groups are a source of deep knowledge.

**Wight territories** — cave-wight and small-folk presences distributed sparsely across the landscape, manifesting as traces, Body Sense alertness, and very rare encounters. The wights are presences, not characters.

**The arriving people** as a cultural phenomenon — a small group of foreigners carrying bronze and alien traditions, mediated by a handful of translators, creating tension and change wherever they go.

**Sex and reproduction** as natural parts of life — partnerships, intimacy, children, the dynamics of desire and alliance. The player may experience intimacy within deep relationships. Children grow into adults. The population changes over time through the balance of birth, death, growth, and decline.

**Disability and difference** present throughout the world's population, understood by the cultures of The Barrow as marks of hidden ability rather than disadvantage. People with physical and cognitive differences hold valued roles, including roles connected to the sacred and the deep knowledge layer.

The habitation layer's fundamental purpose is to ensure that the landscape of The Barrow is not a backdrop but a home — a place where people live, work, trade, love, believe, grieve, celebrate, and carry knowledge that the player can earn the right to share. The world was in motion before the player arrived, and it will continue after they leave.
